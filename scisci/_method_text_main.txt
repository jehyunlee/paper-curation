## Research Timeline: Science of Science (1965–2026) — Full Sub-Category Detail

### STREAM A: Foundations & Metrics of Scientific Impact (1965→2026)
Branches into:
- **"Core Bibliometric Theory & Metrics"** (1965–2010): Establishes foundational concepts like cumulative advantage (`Networks of Scientific Papers`, 1965; `A General Theory of Bibliometric`, 1976), Journal Impact Factor (`Citation Analysis as a Tool`, 1972), and individual metrics like h-index (`An index to quantify an individual's scientific research out`, 2005) and g-index (`Theory and Practice of the g-index`, 2006). Explores universality of citation distributions (2008).
- **"Advanced Impact Analysis & Visualization"** (2009–2020): Develops sophisticated tools and methods for understanding impact, including VOSviewer for visualization (`Software survey: VOSviewer`, 2009), comparing citation approaches (`Co-Citation Analysis Bibliographic Coupling and Direct Citat`, 2010), quantifying long-term impact and "sleeping beauties" (2013, 2015), and introducing the Relative Citation Ratio (RCR) (`Relative Citation Ratio`, 2016). Analyzes citation context (`Visualizing the context of citations`, 2018).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Advanced Impact Analysis & Visualization` **FEEDS INTO** Stream B's `Mapping & Evolution of Scientific Fields` by providing tools like VOSviewer for science mapping.
- **"Standardized & Open Data for Impact"** (2020–2026): Focuses on creating large-scale, standardized, and open data infrastructures for bibliometric research. Includes the development of science-wide author databases (`Updated science-wide author databases`, 2020), open biomedical maps (`Introducing the open biomedical map of science`, 2023), and evaluating metadata quality of open databases like OpenAlex (`OpenAlex in focus`, 2026). Addresses data integration challenges with tools like BibFusion (`BibFusion`, 2026).
Interaction: This sub-stream **MERGES INTO** Stream E's `Large-scale Open Data Infrastructures`, as both contribute to and benefit from the creation of open scholarly data.
- **"Predicting Future Impact & Evolution"** (2024–2025): Explores the use of dynamic network structures and machine learning to predict scientific breakthroughs (`Predicting Scientific Breakthroughs`, 2024) and forecast high-impact research topics (`Forecasting high-impact research topics via machine learning`, 2025) from evolving knowledge graphs.
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Predicting Future Impact & Evolution` **RESPONDS TO** advances in Stream E's `LLM-driven Analysis & Prediction` by adopting machine learning and AI for predictive tasks.

### STREAM B: Structure & Dynamics of Scientific Knowledge (1998→2026)
Branches into:
- **"Network Structure & Collaboration Dynamics"** (1998–2010): Applies complex network theory to scientific systems. Investigates the emergence of scaling laws in random networks (`Emergence of Scaling in Random Networks`, 1999), structure of scientific collaboration networks (`The structure of scientific collaboration networks`, 2001), their evolution as scale-free networks (`Evolution of the social network`, 2002), and community detection algorithms like Louvain (`Fast Unfolding of Communities in Large Networks`, 2008; `Community Detection in Graphs`, 2010). Highlights the increasing dominance of teams in knowledge production (`The Increasing Dominance of Teams`, 2007).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Network Structure & Collaboration Dynamics` **FEEDS INTO** Stream C's `Systemic Inequalities & Diversity Impact` by providing frameworks to analyze social structures in science.
- **"Mapping & Evolution of Scientific Fields"** (2012–2021): Focuses on methods for understanding and visualizing the organization and change of science. Includes the UCSD Map of Science (`Design and Update of a Classification System`, 2012), network approaches to topic models (`A network approach to topic models`, 2018), and integrating text mining with science mapping (`When text mining meets science mapping`, 2020). Develops comparative science mapping (`Comparative science mapping`, 2020) and diachronic embedding for mapping changes (`Mapping the changing structure of science`, 2020).
- **"Innovation, Disruption & Knowledge Frontiers"** (2017–2024): Examines how scientific knowledge advances. Introduces the CD (Consolidation-Disruption) index to measure technological change (`A Dynamic Network Measure of Technological Change`, 2017), explores how unsupervised word embeddings capture latent knowledge (`Unsupervised Word Embeddings`, 2019), and contrasts roles of large vs. small teams in science (`Large teams develop and small teams disrupt`, 2019). Critically analyzes the decreasing disruptiveness of papers and patents over time (`Papers and patents are becoming less disruptive over time`, 2023) and how scientists explore knowledge landscapes (`Science as exploration in a knowledge landscape`, 2024).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Innovation, Disruption & Knowledge Frontiers` **INFORMS** Stream D's `Funding Allocation & Policy Impact` by providing insights into what drives scientific progress.
- **"Conceptual Modeling & Future Mapping"** (2025–2026): Develops advanced conceptual and computational models for large-scale science mapping. Surveys Knowledge Organization Systems (`A Survey on Knowledge Organization Systems of Research Field`, 2025), rethinks thematic evolution in science mapping (`Rethinking Thematic Evolution in Science Mapping`, 2026), and proposes new network growth models incorporating community structure (`The Price-Pareto growth model of networks`, 2026). Aims for scalable mapping of scientific communities (`Mapping scientific communities at scale`, 2025) and identifying interdisciplinary emergence (`Identifying interdisciplinary emergence`, 2024).

### STREAM C: Human Capital & Equity in Science (2009→2025)
Branches into:
- **"Career Trajectories & Productivity"** (2009–2019): Investigates individual scientific careers. Examines the "burden of knowledge" (`The Burden of Knowledge`, 2009), the impact of superstar extinctions (`Superstar Extinction`, 2010; `Does Science Advance One Funeral at a Time`, 2019), and debunks myths about faculty productivity (`The Misleading Narrative`, 2017). Identifies "hot streaks" in creative careers (`Hot streaks in artistic, cultural, and scientific careers`, 2018) and the influence of academic environment (`Productivity Prominence`, 2019), as well as the long-term effects of early-career setbacks (`Early-career setback and future career impact`, 2019).
- **"Systemic Inequalities & Diversity Impact"** (2013–2018): Addresses issues of fairness and diversity in science. Reveals global gender disparities (`Bibliometrics: Global Gender Disparities in Science`, 2013), systemic inequality in faculty hiring networks (`Systematic Inequality and Hierarchy`, 2015), and meta-assesses bias in science (`Meta-assessment of Bias in Science`, 2017). Highlights the preeminence of ethnic diversity in collaboration (`The Preeminence of Ethnic Diversity`, 2018) and the benefits of gender diversity for discovery (`Making gender diversity work`, 2018).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Systemic Inequalities & Diversity Impact` **INFORMS** Stream D's `Funding Allocation & Policy Impact` by highlighting biases in the scientific system.
- **"Intersectional Inequalities & Credit Assignment"** (2020–2022): Deepens the analysis of inequalities, exploring intersectional aspects and credit attribution. Uncovers the diversity-innovation paradox (`The Diversity-Innovation Paradox in Science`, 2020), historical gender inequality in careers (`Historical Comparison of Gender Inequality`, 2020), and unequal impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic (`Unequal effects of the COVID-19 pandemic`, 2020). Observes rising global citation inequality (`Global citation inequality is on the rise`, 2021), intersectional inequalities based on gender and race (`Intersectional inequalities in science`, 2022), and that women are credited less (`Women are credited less in science than men`, 2022).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Intersectional Inequalities & Credit Assignment` **RESPONDS TO** the large-scale data availability from Stream E's `Large-scale Open Data Infrastructures` for detailed analysis.
- **"Mobility, Setbacks & Future Careers"** (2023–2025): Explores the impact of scholar migration patterns (`Global patterns of migration of scholars`, 2023), uses unsupervised embeddings to capture movement trajectories (`Unsupervised embedding of trajectories`, 2023), and investigates the effects of tenure on research trajectories (`Tenure and research trajectories`, 2025). Continues to examine the long-term impact of early career setbacks (`Early career setback and future achievement`, 2025).

### STREAM D: Governance & Integrity of Science Systems (2005→2026)
Branches into:
- **"Reproducibility & Research Misconduct"** (2005–2018): Addresses critical issues of scientific trustworthiness. Argues that "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" (2005) due to research practices. Quantifies misconduct as the majority cause of retractions (`Misconduct Accounts for the Majority of Retracted Scientific`, 2012). Estimates reproducibility of psychological science (`Estimating the Reproducibility`, 2015) and social science experiments (`Evaluating the Replicability`, 2018), and surveys scientists' views on reproducibility (`1,500 scientists lift the lid on reproducibility`, 2016).
- **"Publishing Landscape & Open Science"** (2015–2018): Examines the structure and practices of academic publishing. Highlights the oligopoly of academic publishers in the digital era (`The Oligopoly of Academic Publishers`, 2015), analyzes the prevalence and citation advantage of open access (OA) publications (`The State of OA`, 2018), and identifies the "chaperone effect" in scientific publishing where senior authors boost junior authors' first papers (`The Chaperone Effect`, 2018).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Publishing Landscape & Open Science` **RESPONDS TO** calls for open science and data from Stream E's `Large-scale Open Data Infrastructures`.
- **"Funding Allocation & Policy Impact"** (2014–2022): Investigates how science is funded and its societal impact. Reveals highly unequal collective credit allocation (`Collective Credit Allocation`, 2014) and questions whether peer-review panels select the best ideas (`Big Names or Big Ideas`, 2015). Confirms the Matthew effect in science funding (`The Matthew effect in science funding`, 2018) and analyzes the coevolution of policy and science (`Coevolution of policy and science`, 2021). Demonstrates the broader public use and impact of publicly funded science (`Public use and public funding of science`, 2022).
- **"Future Policy & Science-Society Interface"** (2023–2026): Explores innovative policy approaches and the connection between science and society. Proposes lottery-first funding to reduce bias (`Rethink Funding by Putting the Lottery First`, 2023), maps knowledge for policy applications (`Mapping Knowledge: Topic Analysis of Science`, 2024), and studies partisan disparities in US federal science funding (`Partisan disparities in the funding of science`, 2025). Visualizes the broad impact of science funding (`Funding the Frontier`, 2025), identifies the "strain on scientific publishing" (2024), and examines how libraries bridge the gap between science and society (`Bridging the gap between science and society`, 2026). Also analyzes information pathways in online science communication (`Information Pathways in Online Science Communication`, 2026).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Future Policy & Science-Society Interface` **RESPONDS TO** findings from Stream B's `Innovation, Disruption & Knowledge Frontiers` regarding scientific progress and challenges.

### STREAM E: AI & Computational Methods for Science Studies (2012→2026)
Branches into:
- **"Early Computational Science Mapping"** (2012–2018): Represents early applications of computational methods to classify and map science. This includes the `Design and Update of a Classification System: The UCSD Map o` (2012) and network-based topic models (`A network approach to topic models`, 2018).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Early Computational Science Mapping` **BRANCHES INTO** Stream B's `Mapping & Evolution of Scientific Fields` as it provides foundational techniques for large-scale science mapping.
- **"Large-scale Open Data Infrastructures"** (2020–2023): Focuses on building and utilizing massive, open scholarly datasets as a foundation for Science of Science research. Key developments include S2ORC (`S2ORC: The Semantic Scholar Open Research Corpus`, 2020), OpenAlex (`OpenAlex: A fully-open index`, 2022) following the discontinuation of MAG, and SciSciNet (`SciSciNet: A large-scale open data lake for the science of s`, 2023).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `Large-scale Open Data Infrastructures` **ENABLES** all other streams by providing the essential data backbone for their research.
- **"LLM-driven Analysis & Prediction"** (2023–2025): Explores the direct application of Large Language Models (LLMs) to analyze and predict scientific phenomena. Includes forecasting AI research directions using AI itself (`Forecasting the future of artificial intelligence`, 2023), assessing LLM impact on scientific production (`Scientific production in the era of large language models`, 2025), and utilizing LLMs for knowledge mapping (`What's In Your Field?`, 2025). Emphasizes the role of AI-driven automation as a foundation for next-era SciSci (`AI-Driven Automation Can Become the Foundation of Next-Era S`, 2025) and how LLMs empower SciSci research (`The Empowerment of Science of Science by Large Language Mode`, 2025).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `LLM-driven Analysis & Prediction` **SPAWNS** new methodological avenues for all other streams, offering novel ways to process and understand scientific data.
- **"LLM in Research Evaluation & Policy"** (2025–2026): Investigates the use of LLMs in research evaluation, policy generation, and related areas. Develops benchmarks for LLMs in generating scientific-to-policy briefs (`Sci2Pol`, 2025) and examines stylistic features that fool ChatGPT evaluations (`Which stylistic features fool ChatGPT research evaluations?`, 2026). Also considers the impact of LLMs on federal research funding (`The Rise of Large Language Models and the Direction and Impa`, 2026). Addresses data quality in learning analytics (`Open Datasets in Learning Analytics`, 2026).
Interaction: This sub-stream's `LLM in Research Evaluation & Policy` **FEEDS INTO** Stream D's `Future Policy & Science-Society Interface` by offering new tools and insights for science policy making and evaluation.

### CROSS-CUTTING THREADS
- **Data Infrastructure Evolution:** Progress from early, limited datasets to vast, open, and machine-readable corpora (e.g., Web of Science/Scopus to S2ORC, OpenAlex, SciSciNet).
- **Methodological Sophistication:** A continuous trajectory from basic bibliometric counts to advanced network science, text mining, machine learning, and now Large Language Model (LLM)-driven analytics.
- **Equity & Bias Awareness:** Growing recognition and quantification of systemic inequalities across gender, ethnicity, career stage, and geographic regions, alongside efforts to understand and mitigate their impact.
- **The "Matthew Effect" / Cumulative Advantage:** This concept, where past success begets future success, is a recurrent theme appearing in studies of citation, funding, and career trajectories.
- **The Nature of Innovation & Disruption:** Ongoing research to define, measure, and understand the factors that drive truly novel versus incremental scientific and technological advancements, including the phenomenon of decreasing disruptiveness.
- **Open Science Principles:** A consistent push for greater transparency, accessibility, and reproducibility in all aspects of scientific research and its evaluation, notably through open access publishing and open data initiatives.

### TURNING POINTS
1.  **1960s-1970s: Birth of Quantitative Science Studies & Core Metrics:** The foundational period establishing bibliometrics with Eugene Garfield's citation indexing (Journal Impact Factor) and Derek J. de Solla Price's work on the Matthew effect and science networks, setting the stage for systematic analysis of science.
2.  **2000s: Network Science Revolution & New Metrics:** Application of complex network theory (small-world, scale-free) to scientific collaboration and citation networks, coupled with the emergence of new individual metrics like the h-index, transforming how scientific structure and individual impact were understood.
3.  **2015-2018: Reproducibility Crisis & Calls for Integrity:** Influential works like Ioannidis's "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" ignited widespread concern over research integrity, leading to large-scale reproducibility projects in psychology and social science, and a critical re-evaluation of research practices and biases.
4.  **2018-2022: Rise of Open Data & Disruptive Innovation Metrics:** The emergence of large-scale, open scholarly datasets (S2ORC, OpenAlex) provided unprecedented access to research metadata. Simultaneously, new metrics like the CD index allowed for the quantification of "disruptive" innovation, leading to significant findings about the changing nature of scientific progress.
5.  **2023-Present: AI/LLM Era in Science of Science:** The rapid development and application of Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT marks a transformative period, not only as powerful new tools for analyzing science but also as an object of study regarding their impact on scientific production, evaluation, and policy.

### Executive Summary (Korean)

과학의 과학(Science of Science) 분야는 1965년 초기 계량서지학적 기초 이론과 인용 분석에서 시작하여, 과학 네트워크의 구조와 동학, 그리고 과학의 사회적・윤리적 측면에 대한 심층 연구로 진화해왔습니다. 2000년대에는 h-index, g-index 등 새로운 영향력 지표가 등장하고 복잡계 네트워크 과학이 발전하면서 과학 협업과 팀 연구의 중요성이 부각되었습니다. 2010년대 중반부터는 재현성 위기, 연구 부정행위, 그리고 젠더 및 다양성 불평등 문제가 주요 논의 대상으로 떠오르며 과학 시스템의 건전성과 형평성에 대한 비판적 성찰이 활발해졌습니다. 2020년대에는 S2ORC, OpenAlex, SciSciNet과 같은 대규모 오픈 학술 데이터베이스가 구축되며 연구의 데이터 기반이 크게 확장되었고, 최근 대규모 언어 모델(LLM)의 등장은 AI를 활용한 과학 예측 및 평가 연구라는 새로운 지평을 열며 이 분야의 가장 중요한 동력으로 부상하고 있습니다.

이 분야의 주목할 만한 연구로는 "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" (Ioannidis, 2005)가 현행 연구 관행이 초래하는 재현성 문제를 경고하며 큰 파장을 일으켰습니다. "The Increasing Dominance of Teams in Production of Knowledge" (Wuchty et al., 2007)는 팀 연구가 개인 연구보다 더 큰 영향력을 가진다는 것을 실증하여 과학 협업의 중요성을 강조했습니다. 최근 연구에서는 "Large teams develop and small teams disrupt science and technology" (Wu et al., 2019)와 같이 팀 규모에 따른 과학적 역할의 차이를 밝혀냈으며, "Papers and patents are becoming less disruptive over time" (Park et al., 2023)는 혁신의 감소라는 중요 현상을 정량화했습니다. 특히 OpenAlex (2022)와 같은 개방형 학술 데이터베이스는 연구 분석의 투명성과 접근성을 획기적으로 개선했으며, LLM을 활용해 AI 연구의 미래를 예측하거나(Wang et al., 2023) 과학 정책 브리프를 생성하는(Kim et al., 2025) 등 새로운 연구 방법론의 가능성을 계속해서 제시하고 있습니다.
