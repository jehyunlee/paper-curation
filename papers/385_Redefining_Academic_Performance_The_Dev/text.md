Abstract: The evaluation of academic performance using bibliometric indicators is central to quality assurance, 
promotion, and reward systems in higher education. However, widely used metrics, such as publication counts, 
total citations, and the h-index, face persistent limitations, including selective coverage of scholarly outputs, 
susceptibility to citation manipulation, insufficient adjustment for career length and publication continuity, and 
the uniform treatment of heterogeneous publication types and co-author contributions. To address these issues, 
this study proposes the NK index as a time- and contribution-adjusted composite scholarly performance 
measure that integrates (i) a broader set of citable scholarly outputs, (ii) publication- and citation-type scores, 
(iii) author contribution coefficients, and (iv) academic age and productive duration parameters. By combining 
output volume and citation impact within a single normalised score, the NK index aims to enable fairer 
comparisons across academics with different career trajectories when only bibliometric data are available. 
Keywords: Academic performance measurement, bibliometrics, composite performance index, scholarly 
impact, NK index

However, with the global discourse on publication quality, scientific communities that

Despite its widespread use, the h-index has been subjected to substantial scrutiny

citation manipulation in co-authored articles. In this literature, the terms academic

interchangeably. In line with standard conventions in efficiency analysis, however, we 
distinguish between (i) productivity (output volume over time), (ii) impact (citation-

measured inputs (e.g., resources or labour time, as in DEA/SFA models). Because the

bibliometric performance/impact index rather than an output-to-input efficiency score.

assessing scholars’ publication performance while mitigating the limitations noted above. 
To this end, we propose the NK index, which integrates publication volume and citation 
impact while adjusting for career length (academic age), publication continuity 
(productive duration), and author contribution. The NK index is anticipated to support a 
more realistic and standardised assessment of scholarly publication performance in 
contexts where only bibliometric data are available. 
In this study, productivity refers to the volume of eligible scholarly outputs, whereas 
impact refers to the citation record associated with those outputs. The NK index combines 
these dimensions and normalises them by time and contribution to produce a single 
composite performance score.

2. Literature Review 
2.1 Citation Indexes, Scientific Publications, and Performance Metrics Utilised 
for Evaluating Publication Performance 
Establishing criteria for a more prestigious scientific publication presents significant 
challenges and has been the subject of ongoing debate within the academic community. 
Productivity has emerged as a critical concern in the scientific sphere, as evidenced by the 
recognition accorded to highly productive academics. Over time, the mere act of 
publishing works has ceased to be sufficient, and additional metrics have gained 
prominence for evaluating the significance of scientific contributions. 
Academics engage in diverse activities including the creation of scientific 
publications, participation in social responsibility initiatives, and project management. 
These endeavours were evaluated using various metrics with specific scores and criteria 
established by higher education institutions and regulatory bodies. This framework 
comprises the education, publication, and service categories for academic appointments 
and promotions. However, the education and service aspects are frequently undervalued 
in performance evaluation. Numerous studies also conflate “publication performance,” a 
subset of academic performance, with overall “academic performance.” Consequently,

2.2. Role of Scientific Publications in Performance Measurement 
The significance of accountability has increased concomitantly with an emphasis on 
transparency and competitive dynamics among higher education institutions, particularly 
in North America and Europe. This phenomenon has precipitated the ongoing evolution 
of the performance evaluation criteria for universities. Consequently, universities are 
undergoing restructuring to attain higher rankings and enhance their international 
standing. 
The increasing emphasis on societal responsibility in evaluating academic 
performance has led to the implementation of “qualitative and quantitative performance 
indicators” in higher education, initially introduced by the “Jarrat Report” and now 
globally prevalent. Public and research funding allocation has traditionally relied on the 
scientific publications and research performance of institutions ‘and faculty members’ (Ak 
& Gülmez, 2006, p.24; CoHE, 2004, p.14). Consequently, universities have transitioned 
from traditional evaluation methods, such as course assessments, to prioritising research 
and publication activities, particularly international publications (Demircioğlu, 2014; 
Sgroi & Oswald, 2012; Tonta, 2014; Yenihan, 2015). Thus, three interconnected criteria 
have emerged: “the number of publications in international journals,” “the indexing of 
journals in scientific indexes', and ‘the number of citations of these publications” (Ak & 
Gülmez, 2006, p.22). 
In numerous countries, novel organisations have emerged that evaluate academic 
performance based on specific criteria, resulting in the development of innovative 
methodologies. Universities are globally ranked in educational and research activities 
using various criteria by different institutions, with rankings publicly disseminated 
regularly in Türkiye. Universities that demonstrate excellence in scientific advancement, 
accreditation, and quality in these rankings receive more resources according to 
evaluation criteria. 
Numerous organisations, particularly in the US and Europe, have created indices and 
metrics derived from scientific publications and citations to rank and compare the 
academic performance of academics, departments, universities and nations (Bornmann & 
Leydesdorff, 2014; Waltman, 2016). In Türkiye, the "Entrepreneurial and Innovative 
University Index (EIUI)" developed by TUBITAK the "URAP rankings" established by the 
Middle East Technical University are the two most authoritative ranking systems. URAP 
has ranked Turkish universities since 2009 and world universities since 2010 based on 
academic performance (URAP, 2018). The URAP evaluates universities using nine 
academic performance criteria on a 5-point scale (Article Score, Citation Score, Scientific 
Document Score, Ph.D. Graduate percentage student score, faculty member/student 
score), and the EIUI assesses institutions according to the 23 entrepreneurship and 
innovation criteria. Both the URAP and EIUI rankings assess academic performance based 
on the number of publications in international, indexed journals, the number of 
publications in national, indexed journals, and the citation counts of these publications. 
University rankings, which are closely monitored by administrators, academics, and 
students, demonstrate that institutions in developed countries consistently achieve higher 
rankings owing to scientific advancements, whereas those in developing countries often 
rank lower or fail to meet established criteria. As rating agencies gain increasing influence, 
higher education has become more specialised, emphasising the significance of 
administration. Administrators use rankings to assess their competitive positions and 
implement strategic improvements. The public and student focus on these rankings 
frequently results in criticism of universities from countries not represented on global lists 
or ranking lower domestically. Furthermore, higher education bodies and universities

now prioritise the quantity of scientific publications in the appointment and promotion

In their calculations, various institutions ranked universities using distinct criteria or

education rating agency, to compare universities in Türkiye with international institutions 
on a discipline-specific basis (CoHE, 2018a). 
2.3. Performance indicators used for scholars 
The performance indicators utilised by academics in higher education institutions 
include the number of publications, number of citations, relative citation impact, and 
various metrics. The following sections provide a brief overview of these indicators: 
Number of Publications: 
In evaluating academic performance in higher education, ‘the number of 
publications’ serves as a fundamental performance indicator owing to its quantifiable 
nature and is thus predominantly utilised in assessments. This metric evaluates scientists’ 
academic productivity, while the number of citations measures the impact of this 
productivity within the academic sphere (Al, 2008b, p. 20). These criteria facilitate the 
comparisons and evaluations of scientists, departments, faculties, and universities at the 
regional and national levels. Nevertheless, several challenges arise when these criteria are 
used in academic performance assessments. A primary critique pertains to the fact that 
evaluations based on publication quantity do not incorporate weighting according to 
publication type (e.g., books, book chapters, and articles). 
In Türkiye, the CoHE appointment/promotion criteria and incentive regulations are 
based on weighted evaluations. Assessing an academic’s or academic unit’s performance 
solely by publication quantity may conceal uncited or under-cited works among highly 
cited publications. Additionally, calculating citation counts poses challenges such as 
determining the value of original publications with no or few citations. 
There are qualitative disparities among academic publications across disciplines. On 
the one hand, prestigious journals publish only a small fraction of the submitted articles 
through rigorous peer review processes. Conversely, predatory journals publish all the 
articles submitted for a fee. Nevertheless, substantial variations can be observed, even 
among articles published on the same issue in a given journal. While certain publications 
may attain significant prominence in the literature by making substantial contributions, 
others may have minimal impact. To mitigate the adverse effects of varying publication 
quality, URAP exclusively evaluates articles from high-impact journals. Although the 
journal impact factor serves as a measure of journal quality, it does not guarantee that 
every article in a high-impact journal will be cited nor does it ensure that articles in low-
impact journals receive few or no citations. Similarly, data-based analyses in the ISI Web 
of Science (WoS) databases are commonly employed to assess publication quantity. 
In the "University Monitoring and Evaluation Criteria,” which evaluates universities 
as announced and published by CoHE (2018) in Türkiye, publications and citations are

CoHE (2018b) clarified the assessment strategy for multi-author articles, particularly 
within universities. Guidelines indicate that publications co-authored by faculty from the 
same university are considered a single publication, whereas those by faculty from 
different universities are assessed individually for each institution (CoHE, 2018b). 
Universities must publish these criteria as required by the CoHE; however, there are 
inconsistencies in the evaluation practices among universities and institutions. 
Institutions apply various criteria for multiauthor publications, such as assigning identical 
scores to each author, distributing points equally, giving the first author a higher 
proportion, or scoring authors based on their rank. 
Number of Citations: 
An indicator frequently used to assess the efficacy of publications is the number of 
citations received. The use of software that quantifies citations in determining citation 
counts and even the capability to ascertain the number of citations received from 
publications in the top percentiles are among the significant advancements facilitated by 
technology. The citation count serves as a crucial criterion for evaluating the impact of 
scientific publications. Publications that have made substantial contributions to the 
scientific domain and literature tend to attract numerous citations. However,  the absence 
of citations for a publication does not necessarily indicate a lack of value (Garfield, 1986; 
Sher & Garfield, 1983). 
Several issues arise when citations are used as performance criteria for scientific 
publications. For example, literature review articles tend to attract more citations. 
Historically, low-quality and low-prestige journal publications have struggled to be cited 
(Erkut, 2017, p. 2). However, the Internet and platforms, such as ResearchGate, Academia, 
and Google Scholar, have changed this. In bibliometrics, a publication that goes uncited 
for a long time before suddenly gaining citations is called “sleeping beauty” (Van Raan, 
2004, p. 467). Social Sciences publications may take longer to accumulate citations, 
whereas those in Sciences and Medicine are typically cited within the year of publication. 
Hence, evaluating the citation “half-life” values should be field-specific. Another critique 
involves manipulative behaviours such as self-citation or reciprocal citation. Although 
technology can now exclude self-citations from evaluations, it remains difficult to prevent 
academics from deliberately citing one another. 
Nevertheless, these criteria (publications and citations) have several limitations. 
Erkut (2015) identifies several limitations: due to significant disciplinary variations in 
publication and citation counts, it is essential to consider these differences in academic 
productivity evaluations. Analyses may be flawed by variations in the authors’ names. 
Prolific academics can skew the comparisons of academic units. These criteria can be 
manipulated by those with compromised integrity. Assessing long-established academics 
versus recent entrants is difficult because of the differing academic tenures. These 
measures may also fail to capture the impact of low-citation but highly influential 
publications. Variations in the database scope can produce misleading results when 
certain publications and citations are excluded. Citation criteria may be unreliable due to 
self-citation in co-authored works, and unspecified contribution rates pose challenges. 
Evaluations based only on publications and citation counts have overlooked other 
academic contributions. 
Relative Citation Impact:  
The relative citation effect is a metric frequently employed to compare countries. It is 
calculated by dividing the average citation rate per publication in a country within a 
specific period and subject area by the average citation rate per publication in the

corresponding field globally. A relative citation effect of one indicates that the field in 
question has been cited at a level equivalent to the world average (Al, 2008a, 2008b, p. 34; 
Braun, 1999, p. 428; Katz, 2000, p. 24). 
Expert Opinion and Peer Review:  
In scientifically advanced nations, criteria such as journal impact factors, h-index 
values, and derivatives are not used in isolation to evaluate academic performance, 
appointments, promotions, publication support, or research budget distribution. Expert 
opinions and peer reviews have been consistently sought (Sgroi & Oswald, 2012). 
Influential studies promoting performance indicators based on expert opinions include 
the Research Excellence Framework (REF) in England and the Research Quality 
Framework (RQF) in Australia. The REF uses expert panels to assess research quality and 
influence budget allocations to universities and departments. Subevaluation team 
members, who are experts, are barred from using journal impact factors or rankings 
during publication evaluations (Sgroi & Oswald, 2012). REF accounts for interdisciplinary 
differences in supporting and evaluating academic research (Bekhradnia, 2008; HEFCE, 
2007). The RQF assesses the impact and quality of publicly funded research and guides 
resource allocation. Similarly, some U.S. universities use a committee to evaluate 
academic promotions based on education, research, and services. This committee assesses 
the originality, significance, and impact of a candidate’s research by reviewing a select 
few publications (5-10), rather than the entire body of work (Sgroi & Oswald, 2012, p. 257). 
In Türkiye, as in the United States, peer assessment is used for academic promotion; 
however, the process differs. The CoHE sets the initial criteria, which HEIs then adapt, 
creating directives within legal boundaries to establish specific requirements. Peer 
reviews occur after preliminary commissions approve applications based on these criteria. 
The structure of this system has sparked debates over the objectivity and criticism of the 
academic evaluation process (Demircioğlu, 2014). 
Metrics: h-index:  
Academics have focused on evaluating academic performance and developed 
various indices. One globally recognised index, classified as a 'citation classic,' is the 'h-
index,' introduced by Hirsch in his 2005 publication (Barsotti & Lando, 2015; Hirsch, 2005; 
Zhang et al., 2011). The h-index reflects both productivity and impact by measuring the 
number of publications (h) that each receive at least h citations (Mondal et al., 2023). It is 
recognised for its correlation with prestigious awards, strengthening its reliability as a 
measure of performance. The h-index influences the evaluation outcomes in ISI’s new 
citation report feature on the Web of Science. The elevated h-index values of the Nobel 
Prize and National Academy of Science award recipients corroborate the perceived 
accuracy of the index (Kelly & Jennions, 2006). Furthermore, its extensive application in 
numerous studies has established the h-index to be a globally accepted indicator. In 
Türkiye, URAP, EIUI, and CoHE incorporated the h-index into their calculations. The h-
index quantifies a scientist’s publications and citations and indicates the number of 
publications cited above a specific threshold. It functions as a metric to evaluate 
academics’ scientific activities and citation indices. Although widely employed for 
evaluating scientists, citation counts and the h-index have prompted discussions 
regarding novel methods for assessing scientific publications (Balcı, 2006; Onat, 2006; 
Tunoğlu, 2006). 
Studies indicate that the h-index is utilised not only for scholars but also across 
various fields. It is employed to assess journals (Braun et al., 2006; Schubert and Glanzel, 
2007), research groups (Van Raan, 2006), institutions (Egghe & Rousseau, 2012; Jorge & 
Almauger, 2008; Perme et al., 2012), countries (Csajbok et al., 2007), and publishers 
(Schubert & Glanzel, 2007). Initially proposed on a small scale, h-index evaluations have 
since been adapted to larger datasets. The extant literature on the h-index in Türkiye is 
notably limited (Balcı, 2011).

In response to the criticism of the h-index by academics, numerous alternative indices 
have been developed to address the identified limitations in the field. A substantial body 
of literature has proposed and evaluated alternative measurement methods. Among these 
indices are the g-index (Egghe, 2006), h(2) index (Kosmulski, 2006), A-index (Burrell, 2007; 
Jin, 2006), R-index (Jin et al., 2007), AR index (Jin, 2007; Jin et al., 2007), b-index (Bornmann 
et al. 2007; Warda, 2001), and m-index (Bornmann et al. 2008). 
In the calculation of the A-index value, the mean is computed by summing the "h" 
number of citations utilised in the h-index methodology; for the m-index, the median 
value is employed (Bornmann et al., 2008). Furthermore, in the R-index, the computation 
involves taking the square root of the sum of the "h" number of citations used in the h-
index method. Additionally, the AR index calculation incorporates the age of the 
publications into its model (Jin et al., 2007). These emerging methodologies are primarily 
based on the number of citations to publications. 
Most of these studies used the h-index as their foundation. For instance, a 
measurement method known as the g-index has been developed as an extension of the h-
index. The g-index addresses the h-index's lack of sensitivity to the impact of highly cited 
publications (Aksnes, 2005; Dutta, 2023; Egghe, 2006; Guns & Rousseau, 2009; Orhon et 
al., 2007; Woeginger, 2008), and is defined as the highest number of publications that have 
received no more than g²" or more citations in total. Egghe (2006, p. 143) proposed that 
the g-index can be employed in conjunction with the h-index, asserting that academics 
with a higher "g/h" ratio (Egghe, 2006; Orhon et al., 2007) demonstrate greater scholarly 
impact. In Türkiye, this proposition was adopted, and evaluations were conducted based 
on the "g/h" ratio (Orhon et al., 2007). 
In addition, new indexing studies have been carried out in recent years, such as the 
Advanced Research Quality Index (ERQI) (Mina & Homsi, 2024), which combines several 
performance indicators, and the Ha Index (Fassin, 2023), which provides a time-
normalised approach that increases fairness for young academics. 
2.4. Identification of Limitations: Critiques, Limitations, Deficiencies, and 
Necessity of Developing a Novel Model 
Regarding the subject matter, academics are developing novel indices to address the 
identified deficiencies in the field and are endeavouring to introduce them to scholarly 
literature. Numerous studies have asserted the necessity of enhancing the h-index and 
offering critiques of this metric (Batista et al., 2006; Berker, 2006; Bornmann et al., 2008; 
Costas & Bordons, 2007; Gökçeoğlu, 2006; Jin et al., 2007; Orhon et al., 2007). One of the 
primary concerns underlying these criticisms was the number of citations. However, a 
high citation count is not the sole determinant of an elevated h-index. Consequently, 
academics with few publications but high citation counts tended to have lower h-index 
values. To address this issue, some calculation methodologies have established a 
minimum threshold for the number of publications. 
Hirsch (2005, p. 16571) classifies scientists by their h-index as follows: (i) an h-index 
of 20 after 20 years signifies a successful scientist. (ii) An h-index of 40 after 20 years 
denotes an outstanding scientist, usually associated with top universities or major 
research laboratories. (iii) An h-index of 60 after 20 years or 90 after 30 years identifies 
exceptionally distinguished individuals. Accordingly, an "outstanding scientist" must 
have at least 40 publications with 40 citations (Al, 2008a p. 268). It is unrealistic to expect 
all scientists’ publications to achieve such high citations, implying that scientists with a 
high h-index likely have a higher number of publications. 
In Vinkler’s (2007, p. 3-4) study, including critiques of the subject, the h-index can 
potentially match the number of an individual’s publications, regardless of citation count 
(Al, 2008a, p. 269). It was also found that longer academic careers resulted in higher h-
index values due to the lack of consideration for rarity in evaluations (Bornmann et al., 
2008). Additionally, interdisciplinary variations lead to different h-index values (Batista

111  
 
et al. 2006). Evaluating academics solely by the h-index, without considering total 
citations, excludes certain studies from the measurement (Doğan, 2005). 
It is essential to consider that the h-index exhibits a limited sensitivity to citation 
manipulation. Additionally, the databases employed for the h-index calculation possess 
inherent limitations. For instance, Harzing's Publish or Perish programme computes the 
h-index utilising Google Scholar data, which also automatically calculates the h-index and 
the i10 index in its profile. However, Google Scholar’s database is subject to criticism for 
incorporating nonscientific sources. Consequently, the h-index values derived from it are 
approximately 53% higher than the standard h-index values, lending credence to these 
criticisms (Erkut, 2017). 
A significant issue in measuring individual scholarly performance is that each author 
in a co-authored article receives full citations, analogous to those in single-authored 
publications. To address this concern, Batista et al. (2006) normalised individual citations 
by dividing the total individual h-index by the number of authors. Harzing (2008) 
subsequently introduced an alternative method utilising the "Publish or Perish" software. 
This alternative method normalises the total h-index by dividing the citations by the 
number of authors for each article and then calculates the h-index based on these 
normalised citation numbers. Harzing’s PoP method is much more finely tuned than the 
method of Batista et al., and it is thought to provide a better approximation of the impact 
per author (Harzing, 2008). 
In the NK model, it is proposed that the approach of dividing citations by the number 
of authors is surpassed, and a factor based on the degree of contribution of the academics 
involved in the publications is included in these calculations. For this purpose, the Author 
Contribution Level Coefficient (ACLC; denoted as aclc in the formula) is used. 
To promote sustained scholarly productivity among academics, the concept of 
“continuity of publications” warrants consideration, particularly for academics who 
produce highly cited work but publish it infrequently. Sidiropoulos et al. (2016) describe 
the contemporary h-index (hcont), a time-weighted variant that gives more weight to 
recent publications and down-weights older ones. However, this index, derived from the 
h-index, still fails to address the issue of disregarding the work of certain scientists. 
Similarly, the v-index, which calculates the variation in citations and incorporates a 
productive duration factor, and the m-index, which divides the time since the first 
publication by the h-index to assess continuity, were developed. Nonetheless, neither 
index, based on the h-index framework, resolves the underlying problems. 
In light of the aforementioned factors, it is proposed that the “academic age' be fully 
incorporated into the mathematical model for performance measurement. Subsequently, 
a methodology can be developed to encompass all the cited publications that influence 
the evaluation from the initial year of the study. It is imperative to note that all 
publications of scientific merit should be included in the calculation when assessing 
academic productivity regardless of their citation status. Moreover, given the varying 
significance of individual publications, it is essential to account for these differences in the 
evaluation of publications and citations. Therefore, the current study aimed to develop a 
composite academic performance index model, specifically the NK index, which 
addresses the potential limitations and deficiencies of the existing models. 
3. Methodology 
Following Hirsch (2005), Abramo et al. (2015) and other model studies, this study 
employed a theoretical approach to develop a novel composite scholarly performance 
index, the NK index. The research methodology encompassed the following steps: 
Step 1. Literature review: A comprehensive review of existing academic performance 
metrics was conducted, focusing on citation indices, bibliometric analyses, and various 
bibliometric indices such as the h-index and its variants.

Step 2. Identification of limitations: The study identified several limitations of current 
academic performance evaluation methods, including (i) the selective coverage of 
scholarly outputs (i.e., the exclusion of certain publication types), (ii) susceptibility to 
citation manipulation, (iii) failure to account for academic age and productive duration, 
and (iv) the uniform treatment of all publications and citations regardless of their relative 
contribution or impact. 
Step 3. Model development: To construct the NK index, a mathematical model was 
created by integrating various existing indices and parameters. This model is predicated 
on the logic of previous bibliometric frameworks, allowing for a more nuanced 
understanding of the citation and publication dynamics. 
Step 4. Construction of the formula: A mathematical formula was constructed to calculate 
the NK index, considering all the aforementioned factors. This formula was designed to 
provide a comprehensive and objective measure of academic performance. 
Step 5. Development of a scoring system for the formula: A scoring system was developed 
for different types of publications and citations based on their perceived importance in 
academic assessments. This system was incorporated into NK index calculations. 
Step 6. Comparative analysis and validation: Calculations were performed using data 
from four illustrative academics to assess the effectiveness of the NK index. The results 
were compared with traditional metrics, such as the h-index, to demonstrate the capacity 
of the NK index to overcome the limitations of the existing models. The NK index was 
tested in diverse scenarios to ensure its ability to discriminate between academics with 
similar publication and citation records but different career lengths and productive 
periods. 
The aim of this methodological approach was to create a more comprehensive, 
objective, and standardised evaluation system for assessing scholarly performance, 
address the limitations of existing metrics, and promote sustained scholarly productivity. 
4. Model Formulation and Illustrative Application 
4.1 Model Formulation: NK Composite Scholarly Performance Index (NK) 
Despite numerous studies on academic performance, an objective and 
comprehensive bibliometric indicator that jointly captures output volume, citation 
impact, publication continuity, and author contribution remains limited. To address this 
gap, we propose the NK index as a composite scholarly performance measure. In keeping 
with the standard use of the term efficiency in operations research (outputs relative to 
inputs), we avoid interpreting NK as an output-to-input efficiency score (e.g., in DEA or 
stochastic frontier models). Instead, NK integrates outputs and impact and normalises 
them by academic age, productive duration, and contribution to enable fairer 
comparisons across academics with different career trajectories. 
The NK index considers academics’ contributions to publications and academic 
tenure along with productive duration to promote sustained scholarly output. This 
normalisation supports fairer comparisons between academics with different career 
lengths (e.g., 20-year vs 10-year careers). It is posited that all scientifically meritorious 
publications should be included in assessing an academic’s publication performance 
irrespective of citation status. Consequently, the NK index incorporates all potential 
citations in its calculation, whether cited or not, and encompasses all the received 
citations. Acknowledging that publications vary in significance, the NK index evaluates 
the relative importance of publications and citations based on the categorical criteria 
scores from Sabir Tastan (2020) and assigns scores accordingly.

5. Discussion 
The NK index builds on earlier critiques of the h-index and its variants by integrating 
dimensions that are often treated separately: (i) a broader citable output set with 
publication- and citation-type scores, (ii) citation counts adjusted for contribution and 
citation-distance filtering, and (iii) time/continuity parameters (academic age and 
productive duration). 
The illustrative application with four hypothetical academics shows that NK 
differentiates between identical raw publication/citation totals when career length and 
continuity differ, whereas the h-index may yield similar values. At the same time, NK is 
more data-demanding than simple indices and depends on normative choices (e.g., 
scoring schemes and contribution coefficients). Future research should test robustness 
under alternative scoring settings, across disciplines and empirical datasets, and examine 
potential strategic behaviour. 
6. Conclusion  
The NK index is proposed as a time- and contribution-adjusted composite scholarly 
performance indicator that addresses key limitations of widely used metrics such as the 
h-index. By combining a broader set of eligible outputs with publication- and citation-
type scores, and normalising by academic age and productive duration, NK aims to 
support fairer comparisons across different career trajectories when only bibliometric data 
are available.

filtering and contribution weighting to reduce some of these risks, but complementary 
integrity checks remain necessary.
