Overview of Generative AI GenAI is a branch of artificial intelligence that produces new content, such as text, im- ages, and other mixed-format outputs.. Its main aim is to generate coherent material rather than only analyze, label, or sort existing data.. This section reviews GenAI and outlines its development from early probabilistic approaches to current large-scale foundation models.. This paper defines the key concepts, traces the field’s development through major model ar- chitectures, and reviews the current set of widely used models and their applications.. This background provides the context needed for the architectural, methodological, and ethical discussions in the next sections.. Definition and Concepts Generative artificial intelligence (AI) represents a paradigm shift within machine learning, defined by systems capable of synthesizing novel, plausible data, including text, images, audio, code, and multimodal content, that reflect the underlying distribution of their training data.. Unlike discriminative models, which focus on learning the conditional probability P(y | x) to map inputs to labels or categories, generative models learn the joint probability distribution P(x) of the data itself, enabling them to generate new samples [22].. This distinction is not only technical; it also matters in practice.. Discriminative models are commonly used for classification and regression because they learn to map inputs to target outputs.. Generative models learn the data distribution, which supports tasks such as producing new content, augmenting training data, and simulating complex settings.. The recent rise of GenAI is closely tied to the development of foundation models, which are large models pretrained on broad, often multimodal, datasets and can later be adapted for many downstream tasks [22].. These models are trained with self-supervised methods on very large text collections, which helps them learn patterns and internal representations that transfer across different tasks and fields.. These models can generate new outputs and are not restricted to copying existing material.. They show emergent properties, in- cluding coherence within context, adaptation to different styles, and reasoning across modalities [23].. The move from narrow, task-bound generators to general generative sys- tems points to a basic change in AI’s role, shifting from analytic tools to collaborators in producing new work.. Historical Evolution The evolution of GenAI has been driven by successive architectural paradigms that progressively improved generation quality, stability, and scalability, enabled by advances in algorithms, data availability, and computational resources.. • Generative adversarial networks (GANs): Introduced by Goodfellow [24], GANs enable high-quality images through adversarial training, but training often becomes unstable and can lead to mode collapse, which limits how well the approach scales to diverse, multimodal data.. https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 8 of 46 • Variational autoencoders (VAEs): VAEs offer a stable probabilistic approach to learning latent representations, but their samples are often less sharp and detailed than those produced by GANs, which limits their use in settings that required high-fidelity image generation [25].. • Autoregressive large language models (LLMs): Transformer-based architectures [26] have shown that training large models for next-token prediction at scale can yield strong generative outputs and improved reasoning, which has made scalability a central design principle.. • Diffusion models: Diffusion models generate data by starting from random noise and repeatedly removing it [27].. This process is often stable, yields diverse samples, and supports controllable image synthesis, which is why it is widely used in text-to- image generation [28].. • Multimodal foundation models: Recent multimodal models combine text and images using contrastive learning and cross-attention [29].. This approach supports tasks ranging from zero-shot image recognition to few-shot multimodal reasoning, as shown in systems such as Flamingo and GPT-4V.. Research has moved from adversarial and variational approaches to scalable autore- gressive and diffusion models, and it now focuses on unified multimodal foundation models that reflect current best performance in the field.. Autoregressive Transformer-based models have dominated large-scale language mod- eling from 2020 to 2025.. Trained via next-token prediction with self-attention, models such as GPT-4, LLaMA 3, and Gemini exhibit strong reasoning, dialogue, and code synthesis capabilities, supported by in-context learning and predictable scaling behavior [34].. Se- quential generation can increase latency, raise the risk of hallucinations, increase inference costs, and create alignment issues, especially in safety-critical settings [34].. Even with these constraints, autoregressive Transformers continue to serve as the main architecture behind most language systems in practical use.. Diffusion models such as Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 3, and Sora generate images and videos by repeatedly denoising a sample over many steps, which supports stable training and high-quality outputs [35].. Diffusion models are widely used for text-to-image generation, image editing, and super-resolution because they offer good control over composition and reduce mode collapse, even though they often require slow inference, high memory use, and still struggle with temporal consistency [35].. Multimodal foundation models such as GPT-4V, Flamingo, and Gemini 1.5 integrate text, vision, audio, and video through cross-attention or fusion mechanisms, enabling strong zero-shot and few-shot cross-modal transfer [36].. Although these models allow more natural interaction and embodied reasoning, they still depend on very large datasets and high compute, raise open questions about how to evaluate them, and may carry forward biases across modalities [36].. Generative adversarial networks, represented by StyleGAN 3 and BigGAN-deep, remain effective for high-resolution image synthesis with fast inference [37].. In practice, training instability, limited controllability, and weak scalability to multimodal data have kept GANs largely confined to niche uses such as portrait generation and artistic style transfer [37].. In addition to the models discussed above, recent developments have introduced GPT-5 as the next-generation large-scale foundation model in the GPT series.. While official technical doc- umentation remains limited due to its proprietary nature, early reports indicate improved performance in complex reasoning, multimodal understanding, and instruction-following compared to GPT-4-class systems.. As with other closed-source models, full architectural details, training datasets, and alignment methodologies are not publicly disclosed, which constrains independent empirical verification.. Nevertheless, GPT-5 represents a continua- tion of the scaling and integration trends that characterize the foundation model era.. Foundation Model Architectures Foundation models are characterized by architectural paradigms that enable gener- alization and cross-domain reasoning.. Modern GenAI primarily relies on Transformer, diffusion-based, and multimodal architectures, which together define the core design space of contemporary large-scale generative models.. These equations summarize canonical definitions widely adopted in the literature and are included to support reproducibility at the architectural level rather than to introduce novel derivations.. Transformer-Based Architectures The Transformer architecture represents a significant paradigm shift in deep learning by discarding the use of recurrence and convolution in favor of fully attention-based mechanisms for modeling sequential data.. In contrast to recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and their gated extensions, such as long short-term memory (LSTM) and gated recurrent unit (GRU), which process input tokens sequentially, the Transformer enables parallel processing of all sequence elements.. This design substantially enhances training efficiency and scalability.. Central to the architecture is the self-attention mechanism, which enables each token to dynamically attend to all other tokens in the sequence, thereby facilitating the modeling of both local and long-range dependencies within the data.. Encoder–Decoder Paradigm The Transformer framework is commonly organized around an encoder–decoder architecture.. In this structure, the encoder processes the input sequence and maps it into a high-dimensional latent representation, while the decoder generates the output sequence in an autoregressive manner.. Depending on the model’s design, three main configurations are typically distinguished: • Encoder-only models: Primarily optimized for understanding and classification tasks (e.g., BERT), these models rely on bidirectional context encoding to capture semantic relationships within the input.. • Decoder-only models: Intended for generative or autoregressive applications such as text completion (e.g., the GPT family), these architectures predict the next token sequentially based on preceding context.. • Encoder–decoder models: Applied to sequence-to-sequence problems like translation and summarization (e.g., T5 and BART), these models integrate the strengths of both components to align input and output representations.. Scaling Innovations in Transformer Models Recent research has shown that the performance of Transformer models follows a predictable trend as model size, dataset scale, and computational capacity increase.. This relationship is commonly characterized by the following scaling law: L(N, D, C) ∝N−αN + D−αD + C−αC, (5) where L denotes the model loss, N represents the number of parameters, D the dataset size, and C the available compute budget.. Large-scale Transformer architectures such as GPT-3, PaLM, and LLaMA have capitalized on these scaling dynamics by employing hundreds of billions of parameters.. To mitigate the resulting computational overhead, these systems adopt architectural refinements like sparse activation schemes and mixture- of-experts (MoE) layers, which enable selective computation and greater efficiency at scale.. Collectively, these advances have solidified the Transformer’s role as the cornerstone of contemporary GenAI, supporting diverse applications across natural language processing, computer vision, and multimodal reasoning.. Diffusion-Based Generative Models Diffusion-based generative models have become a widely used approach in GenAI, with strong results on many synthesis tasks such as creating images, videos, and audio.. Au- toregressive and adversarial methods usually generate samples by mapping random noise straight into the data space, while diffusion models learn the reverse of a step-by-step noising process.. In this approach, unstructured noise is gradually refined into coherent, high-quality outputs through repeated denoising steps.. These models build on non-equilibrium thermo- dynamics and probabilistic modeling, where data generation is treated as a parameterized diffusion denoising process that the system learns during training.. Unified Classification Framework and Cross-Field Comparison Dimensions GenAI and foundation models (FMs) have developed rapidly through their applica- tion in natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision (CV) and robotics and biomedicine, which has resulted in multiple separate systems that use different domain- specific classification systems.. The existing requirement for a single unified view can be resolved through the creation of a cross-disciplinary classification system which combines architectural advancements and training methods and operational functions into standard measurement dimensions.. The framework permits users to compare model performance and system perfor- mance between different research domains [113–115].. Foundation model systems require four distinct taxonomic categories to represent their complete operational life cycle and their system performance capabilities.. The first classification system for models sepa- rates them into two groups based on their current development status and their methods for adapting to new situations [114].. The base foundation models, which include GPT and LLaMA, function as general-purpose backbones because they contain large-scale ar- chitectures that have undergone pretraining on extensive and diverse datasets [114,116].. Instruction-tuned variants undergo two different training methods, supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which help them achieve better performance in following specific task instructions and meeting human expecta- tions [114].. Multimodal variants build upon the basic premise of multimodal information systems by creating/processing and generating several types of information concurrently (i.e., using text, images, sound at the same time) with shared latent representations across the different types of information or separate modality-specific adapters [117–119].. The second dimension refers to the type of system design (i.e., the actual phys- ical design of each of these systems and/or their function within systems) [113,120].. In one type of system, FMs may be the main units of perception; therefore, they will be performing feature extraction, object identification and interpretation of sensory data within the scope or region of CV and robotics use cases [121,122].. In contrast, under another type of system configuration, they may, instead, function mostly as generative engines synthesizing all types of data such as text in an NLP use case and images or video in https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 30 of 46 an AI-generated use case (AIGC) [121,123].. Nevertheless, in more complex examples of multimodal systems, FMs can be considered within the context of agentic frameworks where FMs could act as planners, coordinators, or decision-makers capable of interfacing with other tools or APIs (application programming interfaces) to complete more sophis- ticated/perhaps complex tasks [120,122].. Systems developed using unified architectures integrate both the perception and generation functions of FMs into a single monolithic or modular end-to-end system and allow for a closer interdependencies between sensory function and reasoning and action [115,121].. The third dimension focuses on operational modality and unifiability concerns [29,115].. Single-modality models are designed specifically to work with one particular type of infor- mation (e.g., they may be used only for analyzing clinical text or medical images) [124,125].. In contrast, cross-modal alignment models map disparate sources of data into a common latent space so that the models can jointly reason about data.. One example of a cross-modal alignment model is CLIP, which aligns representations from images and words [119,123].. At the most advanced level, Generalist Multimodal AI (GMM) architecture emphasizes the concepts of modularity, extensibility, and adaptability, allowing them to include new modalities and tasks with minimal retraining [115,126], and they incorporate responsible- AI-by-design principles as an added fourth dimension.. The governance of pretraining involves dataset curation and filtering strategies designed to reduce bias and improve safety [113].. Safety alignment includes post-training interventions aimed at mitigating harmful or undesirable outputs, such as red-teaming and safety-constrained reinforcement learning [114,119].. In addition, system-level controls include architectural safeguards, such as filtering, monitoring, and transparency mechanisms, that are built into FM-based deployments to ensure compliance with ethical and regulatory expectations [113] across all domains.. Researchers can evaluate models across all domains based on a consistent set of evaluation dimensions.. Benchmark performance and task generality will be assessed according to domain-specific task suites, such as GLUE in NLP and ImageNet in CV, to determine accuracy and reasoning ability [117,121,124].. The zero-shot and few-shot performance also reflects their generalization abil- ity, as they have shown successful completion of tasks without task-dependent fine- tuning [114,122].. The number of parameters updated through base model adaptation for downstream tasks shows their ability to adapt and transfer to other domains.. This has been shown through low-rank adaptation (LoRA) to complete downstream tasks using a low amount of domain-specific data [115,122,127].. In robotics and healthcare, the robustness and real-world grounding metrics evaluate their ability to resist distribu- tion shifts and determinewhether their digital abstraction has a direct correlation to the physical and/or clinical reality they relate to, respectively [113,125,126].. Efficiency and scalability metrics evaluate the computational power, memory requirements, and inference time necessary to achieve real-time performance; these are very important when building edge devices [114,123,127].. The alignment and responsible AI metrics assess the degree to which the model adheres to human-centric principles, indicators of fairness, privacy, explainability, and the standards of safety for each domain, particularly in clinical sit- uations [113,114,116,125,128,129].. Unified Cross-Domain Evaluation Framework for Foundation Models Continuous development of foundational models (e.g., natural language processing, computer vision, etc.) across various modalities provides insight into the current limitations of fragmented and singular benchmarking approaches.. Because of the rapid expansion of foundational models into high-stakes applications, the need for a more unified and holistic evaluation system is increasingly evident.. One way to address concerns regard- ing incomplete discussions surrounding evaluation strategies is through the creation of a modular framework for cross-domain evaluation, which will allow for standardized evaluations that still meet the needs of each domain.. Our proposed framework contains six key components in order to establish an overarching evaluative framework for GenAI: task performance; safety/harm; bias/fairness; robustness; efficiency/cost; and factual- ity/explanation (see Table 9).. This evaluation framework will draw from many recent frameworks (i.e., HEMM, ChEF, GAICo) in order to expand upon our current understand- ing of evaluating GenAI.. To date, there has been no way to assess foundational models based on multiple criteria, which limits comparability between various foundational models and creates uncertainty regarding deployment in application areas (e.g., healthcare and robotics) that carry signifi- cant risks and/or potential trade-offs.. To fill the voids identified in previous frameworks, the proposed framework provides explicit axes of evaluation that represent capabilities, risks, and operational costs [130,131].. Standardized measurement and reporting practices allow meaningful comparison across domains and enable the evaluation of models across not only performance but also safety, fairness, and robustness [132,133].. Although task performance has traditionally served as the baseline dimension against which performance https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 32 of 46 is evaluated through task-specific benchmarks and capability suites [130,131,133], task performance goes beyond simply measuring a model’s ability to perform on tasks.. Performance, in the case of natural language processing (NLP), includes the capa- bilities for reasoning, summarizing, and answering questions [130,133].. In multimodal systems, performance consists of evaluating the fine detail of image understanding and alignment [131,133].. Clinical performance in the practice of medicine relies on accuracy, completeness, reliability and soundness of reasoned clinical decisions [134,135]; however, it does not adequately reflect safety considerations for high-risk industries without combin- ing performance with complementary measures related to risk.. Evaluating the safety of a given model includes the objective of eliminating the potential for producing an unsafe or unreliable output, especially within high-risk industries [130,133].. In healthcare, clinical frameworks provide clinicians with tools to detect hallucinations, assess refusal behavior, and mitigate risk of making an unsafe or unreliable clinical decision [134].. With respect to the robotics platform, an ideal safety evaluation framework would include the incor- poration of closed-loop failure analysis and simulation-based hazard testing methods, although there is limited availability of standardized approaches and guidelines at this time [136].. In addition to determining whether a model produced a reliable or an unreliable output, determining whether or not the outputs produced by a given model are biased or fair is essential in medicine and healthcare economic outcomes research (HEOR).. Eth- ical audits, demographic parity analyses, and continual monitoring of fairness must be performed regularly to ensure that potential sources of bias in decision making do not affect healthcare or HEOR clinical outcomes [133,134,137].. Although the fields of natural language processing and medicine have seen progress in measuring fairness metrics, there is very little research on how to measure fairness with regards to robotic systems.. Robust- ness is measured by stability to changes in distributions, adversarial prompting, or using multistep reasoning for stress tests on robotic systems [131,133].. The idea of multimodal robustness considers whether the robot can keep working correctly while being exposed to sensory noise or occlusion [131].. In clinical settings, methods of cross-examining truth- fulness can be used to evaluate factual consistency across multiple situations [134,138].. As models increase in size, efficiency and cost have become important in terms of eval- uating their use by measuring latency, memory usage, and the trade-offs of resources to performance [137,139,140].. These issues will be especially important in robotics that operate in real time or in screening patients at large clinics [137,139].. The determination of whether a model is factual is done using methods such as hallucination detection and grounding tests [133,141].. Methods that can be broadly applied, like “LLM juries”, which consist of combining the output from many models or expert humans, can be used to evaluate the fac- tuality of complex tasks like clinical summaries [135,138].. Computational and Energy Requirements Many foundation models are built by training at very large scales, with billions of parameters and datasets that are also very large.. Scaling can improve performance, but it requires large amounts of compute and funding, which limits state-of-the-art development to a small number of organizations [22].. Large-scale training can also have a substantial environmental impact, producing carbon emissions on the order of what would be gen- erated over the lifetimes of several cars, especially when the electricity supply relies on carbon-intensive sources [143,144].. Deploying the model at scale tends to increase the energy used for inference.. These concerns have motivated research on efficiency methods, including quantization, pruning, sparsity, and mixture-of-experts architectures [145], but it remains unclear how to obtain large efficiency gains without reducing performance.. Addressing computational and energy challenges requires a combination of architec- tural and operational strategies.. Hardware-aware training, sparse attention mechanisms, adaptive computation routing, and modular mixture-of-experts designs can significantly re- duce redundant computation.. Furthermore, quantization-aware training and post-training compression techniques allow lower-precision inference without substantial performance degradation.. The integration of renewable energy sources in large-scale data centers and transparent reporting of energy consumption metrics should also become standard practice to ensure sustainable deployment of GenAI systems.. Ethical Concerns Ethical risks can arise when models are trained on large, uncurated datasets that contain societal biases, which may lead to discriminatory outcomes in downstream applications [146].. Generative models can also produce hallucinations, where they generate fluent text that is false, which creates risks in areas such as healthcare and education [147].. The capacity to produce realistic synthetic media raises concerns about misinformation, deepfakes, and reduced trust in digital content [148].. Copyright and intellectual property disputes can make deployment more difficult because training may rely on copyrighted material without clear permission from rights holders.. Mitigation strategies for ethical concerns include rigorous dataset documentation, bias auditing using standardized fairness benchmarks, and continual post-deployment monitoring.. Retrieval-augmented generation can reduce hallucination risks by grounding outputs in verified external knowledge sources.. Additionally, structured red-teaming, adversarial testing, and alignment tuning through reinforcement learning from human feedback can enhance ethical robustness.. Establishing clear data provenance policies and transparent model cards further strengthens accountability and responsible AI governance.. Safety and Robustness GenAI systems can still be targeted through adversarial inputs and prompt manip- ulation that evade built-in safety controls [149].. Alignment methods that rely on human feedback can still allow misaligned behavior, especially in new or unclear settings [22].. Se- curity risks include producing code with weak security and supporting social engineering attacks [150].. Performance varies across languages, cultures, and rare edge cases, which suggests a need for stronger alignment and clearer safety guarantees.. Improving safety and robustness requires multilayered safeguards.. These include adversarial training pipelines, structured stress-testing under simulated edge cases, and closed-loop evaluation environments for high-risk applications such as robotics and healthcare.. Neuro-symbolic reasoning integration and constraint-based decoding can enhance logical consistency and reduce unsafe outputs.. Furthermore, incorporating un- https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 35 of 46 certainty estimation and human-in-the-loop validation mechanisms provides additional safeguards in safety-critical domains.. Data Privacy Web-scale pretraining can create privacy risks because a model may memorize and later reproduce sensitive or proprietary information [151].. These risks are higher in reg- ulated settings and can increase through user interactions with deployed systems [8].. Methods such as differential privacy and federated learning can reduce some privacy risks, but they often lower model performance, so privacy-preserving GenAI remains an unresolved research problem.. Advances in privacy-preserving GenAI should focus on improving the trade-off between data utility and confidentiality.. Differential privacy mechanisms can be optimized through adaptive noise calibration, while federated learning frameworks can incorporate secure aggregation and encrypted gradient exchange.. Synthetic data generation, when properly validated, may reduce reliance on sensitive datasets.. Clear governance policies, privacy audits, and compliance with regulatory standards such as GDPR and HIPAA are essential for responsible real-world deployment.. Evaluation Metrics Common metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, and FID do not reflect important aspects of model quality, such as factual accuracy, reasoning ability, safety, and bias [152].. This constraint is even stronger for multimodal models because the available benchmarks are scattered and often do not reflect real-world settings.. Although human evaluation can provide useful insight, it is expensive and does not scale well.. As benchmarks become saturated, reliability tends to decrease, which supports the need for broader, more resilient evaluation frameworks [153].. To improve evaluation practices, future research should adopt unified cross-domain evaluation frameworks that incorporate multidimensional metrics including reliability, safety, robustness, fairness, efficiency, and hallucination detection.. Standardized benchmark suites aligned with real-world deployment contexts should complement traditional task- based metrics.. Hybrid evaluation strategies that combine automated scoring, structured factuality probes, and expert human assessment can enhance validity.. Transparent reporting of dataset characteristics, statistical variance, and experimental configurations will further strengthen reproducibility and cross-study comparability.. Future Directions As GenAI and foundation models improve, future research should move beyond scaling for higher benchmark results and focus on efficiency, clear reporting of methods and limits, generalization across different data types, and broader access with appropriate safeguards.. Large-scale models can perform well, but their lasting value will depend on how research addresses limits in sustainability, interpretability, and fair access.. This section describes the main research directions that are guiding the development of the next generation of foundation models.. Efficient Training and Inference A central future direction is to develop training and inference methods that reduce compute demands, lower energy use, and limit environmental impact.. Many current foundation models depend mainly on brute-force scaling, and this approach is becom- ing less sustainable over time.. Methods such as quantization, pruning, and knowledge distillation can reduce model size and inference time while keeping accuracy close to that of the original model.. Quantization lowers numeric precision to reduce memory https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 36 of 46 use and speed up inference [154].. Pruning removes redundant parameters or attention heads to cut computation [155].. Knowledge distillation trains a smaller student model to match the behavior of a larger teacher model, which supports deployment in resource- limited settings [156].. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning approaches, such as adapters and low-rank adaptation, can make foundation models easier to adapt and personalize for specific tasks [157].. Future work will likely combine these methods with adaptive model designs and hardware-aware efficiency constraints to support sustainable GenAI while maintaining model quality and speed.. Improved Interpretability and Explainability Even when foundation models perform well, their internal reasoning is often unclear, which can reduce trust, hinder error analysis, and complicate use in safety-critical settings such as healthcare and law.. Current interpretability methods, such as attention visual- izations, probing classifiers, and concept attribution, can provide some insight, but they still do not fully account for how the model reaches its decisions [158].. Mechanistic inter- pretability studies the internal circuits of neural networks and is still an early-stage area of research [159].. Explainability matters for multimodal and agentic systems because their reasoning can move across different data types and unfold over many time steps.. Develop- ing interpretable model architectures and standard explanation frameworks is necessary to meet regulatory requirements, support human oversight, and enable the responsible deployment of AI systems.. Multimodal Foundation Models Beyond Text and Vision Most current multimodal foundation models combine text and images, but later systems are likely to include audio, video, sensor inputs, spatial data, and data from embodied interaction.. Recent studies examine joint language–audio–vision models for video understanding, speech-based interaction, and robotics [160].. Beyond perception alone, embodied AI and autonomous agents in physical settings need to combine haptic input, proprioception, and signals from the environment to support reliable action [161].. As multimodal systems expand, they face practical issues with aligning and synchronizing data across modalities, as well as with assessing performance, which calls for scalable fusion methods and reliable cross-modal reasoning for use in real-world settings.. Human–AI Collaboration and Agentic AI Systems A major area for research is human–AI collaboration, especially the design of agentic AI systems that can plan over long time spans, reason through complex tasks, and take actions toward defined goals.. Future foundation models are often described not as passive tools but as collaborative partners that can support problem solving, creative work, and de- cision making.. Recent work on tool-augmented language models and autonomous agents suggests that these systems can interact with external systems, run code, and divide tasks into smaller steps [162].. Agentic behavior raises challenges for reliability, controllability, and alignment.. Future research should focus on safe agent design, human-in-the-loop over- sight, methods for representing uncertainty, and fail-safe controls so that greater autonomy supports human decision making rather than weakening it.. Democratization and Open-Source Foundation Models Making foundation models more widely accessible is an important step toward AI research and deployment that includes diverse groups and supports fairness.. The growing dominance of proprietary models raises questions about transparency, accountability, and unequal access to these systems.. Open-source foundation models offer an alternative because they allow more researchers to take part in study, review and audit the models, https://doi.org/10.3390/bdcc10030094 Big Data Cogn.. 2026, 10, 94 37 of 46 adapt them to specific needs, and deploy them in practice [163].. They support reproducible research and allow low-resource communities to adapt models to local languages and specific needs.. At the same time, open access can increase the risk of misuse and create security concerns.. Future work should balance openness and responsibility by using clear governance frameworks, careful licensing choices, and coordinated work between researchers, industry partners, and public officials.. Conclusions This review has systematically examined the evolution of GenAI and foundation models, with a focus on their underlying architectures, training strategies, and emerging application domains.. Recent work on Transformer LLMs, diffusion generative models, and multimodal systems has shifted research toward general-purpose AI that can learn reusable representations and perform many tasks with limited supervision.. Combining large-scale self-supervised pretraining with efficient fine-tuning has allowed foundation models to reach strong results in natural language processing, computer vision, robotics, and biomedical tasks.. Even with recent progress, several key challenges still remain.. Training and running very large models often requires high levels of computing power and electricity, which raises concerns about environmental impact and fair access to these systems.. Unresolved issues, including limited interpretability, vulnerability to bias and misinformation, risks to data privacy, and inconsistent evaluation methods, still constrain the safe and trustworthy use of GenAI in real-world contexts.. Despite its broad coverage and systematic structure, this survey is subject to certain limitations.. In particular, empirical reproducibility within the reviewed literature is often constrained by the proprietary nature of many state-of-the-art foundation models and the restricted accessibility of their training datasets, alignment strategies, and evaluation pipelines.. Consequently, several reported performance outcomes are derived from closed- source systems that cannot be independently examined or fully replicated.. This reliance on partially undisclosed industrial platforms contributes to a reproducibility gap within the field and limits the extent to which empirical findings can be directly validated within the scope of this review.. Future work should move away from improvements based only on scaling and focus instead on model design that is efficient, interpretable, and responsible.. Future research should focus on parameter-efficient model designs, stronger multimodal reasoning across more than text and images, alignment and safety methods with clear guarantees, and shared evaluation standards that match practical use settings.. Alongside technical progress, open and collaborative research efforts are needed to support transparency, reproducibility, and responsible broad access to foundation models.. Addressing these challenges is nec- essary to ensure that GenAI systems develop in ways that meet scientific standards and support public interests..
