Research funding determines the course of science and thus shapes future knowledge. However, funding allocation is inherently biased, non-optimal and costly. We present a Shiny app that simulates the effects of funding scenarios on costs, diversity and quality. We advocate a lottery at the beginning to promote inclusion.

As bias “… is something we’d like to avoid”, as Matthias Egger (president of the Swiss National Science Foundation (SNSF)) asserts, a broadening of the debate on the models of research funding by stakeholders is needed. To counteract biases, it has been suggested that a lottery as a ‘tiebreaker’ in the final decision round could help to increase decision quality and reduce discrimination. TheSNSF has already implemented the idea of a random-selection process for awarding grants in the final stage of the decision process, as has the GermanVolkswagen Foundation. The latter found that researchers were in favour of the lottery step because it enabled riskier proposals and reduced perceived biases.

Peer review and grant allocation decisions are poorly predictive of overall grant productivity1. One reason for this is that humans have persistent biases that affect their judgments2. This is also true for reviewers when they evaluate the quality of grants. To improve the distribution of research funding to researchers with the highest potential, including those from historically marginalized communities (for example, female researchers, early-career researchers, researchers with a migration background, researchers with disabilities, researchers of colour and other groups), we need to be more radical. We argue that lotteries introduced at the beginning of the grant application process will be more effective at allocating grants than existing models (Fig.1). To simulate the effects of funding scenarios on costs, diversity and quality, we introduce the Shiny app ‘GrantInq’ (Fig. 2; available online at OSI Lübeck, and see GitHub for code). This app is designed for use by researchers, policymakers and funding agencies in response to a recentcall that “researchers (and funders) need to test other models for grant allocation” to evaluate their success in increasing the diversity of applicants. GrantInq provides an accessible framework to explain the assumptions that underlie funding scenarios and to model their downstream effects.

Next, the initial review phase might be subject to biases that the tiebreaker lottery ought to avoid (for example, review bias, systematic offset, language bias and disciplinary bias)4. For example, a study in 2019 found that applicant-appointed reviewers were four times more likely to provide positive feedback than those selected by the agency thus implying that researchers with larger networks might face more beneficial reviews. The opposite tendency — namely, the rejection of high-quality grants owing to nonfinancial conflicts of interest (for example, cronyism, affinity bias, topic choice or geographical bias) can also creep into all phases of the evaluation process when not explicitly addressed5.

How can such biases be overcome and members of underprivileged groups obtain equal opportunities to participate in the grant application process (a process that is almost impossible to blind)?

As one possible intervention, we advocate the establishment of lotteries right at the beginning of the grant application process. For a given research area, we propose an automated selection process (for example, all researchers with a PhD in that area) or a call for enrolment (for example, join at the click of a button) in which all candidates are entered into the lottery at the first stage of the process without having to spend any resources writing a grant proposal (that is, avoiding entry bias).

#### ‘Pre-lotteries’ can counter bias

Our simulations show that well-known and often-described biases are already in effect before a tiebreaker lottery kicks in at the very end of the process. Grant applicants must have passed various forms ofsystemic barriersthroughout the grant submission and decision-making process to actually reach a potential tiebreaker. This begins with the initial decision to apply for a grant (that is, self-selection), which varies between members of social groups owing to lifelong established socialization

If a researcher is randomly selected, they move to the next phase in which they have time to formulate and submit their proposal (or opt out of the process). In this way, the application process is reversed. Instead of self-applications (as in the traditional model), researchers

in this model may formulate and submit their proposals only if they are drawn in the ‘pre-lottery’ (see also the US Diversity Immigrant Visa Program — or ‘Green Card lottery’ — for an example of an additional insertion of quotas before the lot).

new and exploratory ideas. We simulated four exemplary funding scenarios and here compare their benefits and drawbacks with respect to costs, diversity and quality. We contrasted a one-stage scenario (for example, the classic National Institutes of Health Research Project Grant R01 or the Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft), a two-stage scenario (for example, the European Research Council (ERC)), the tiebreaker lottery (for example, SNSF) and the proposed pre-lottery scenario, and we transparently model various assumptions that affect the success of grant applications. All modelled scenarios result in improved quality of selected grants. However, when considering the costs, sunk costs, effects of self-selection and different biases in each additional review round, we can see that the pre-lottery scenario may have many advantages over others (Fig.2). Here, our simulations illustrate the potential value of pre-lotteries in the grant application process in freeing up substantial resources, which could again be used to improve the review process (for example, bytraining reviewers in order to reduce bias).

This reversed process will come with another advantage: much higher efficiency. Currently, a huge number of proposals are rejected with considerable malinvestments of the time, effort and money of the academic workforce and institutions6. In a recent contest model for scientific funding competitions, it was shown that the effort researchers waste in grant writing is comparable to the total scientific value of the funded research, highlighting the illogical nature of the funding process7. However, these costs might not deter researchers to the same extent. The subjective or relative costs in the event of rejection are greater for marginalized groups and researchers who structurally have less access to resources, ultimately leading members of these groups to submit fewer proposals — introducing further bias. Additionally, comparable malinvestments are also experienced by reviewers, who spend most of their resources on applications that are ultimately rejected.

To rethink funding, we thus urge funding agencies to increase the transparency of their funding allocation processes in terms of diversity, costs and research quality. Simulation of different biases in grant programmes and modelling the effect of more systematic changes to the procedures will advance the discussion of grant allocations as a governance tool of scientific progress.

In addition, we propose that the funding for all draws of the pre-lottery should be secured in advance — that is, a lack of money should not be an argument for the rejection of a promising application. We thus suggest a normative review (similar to peer review in journal publications) rather than reviewers making a decision in a competitive process that is more likely to be influenced by the aforementioned biases8. Moreover, secured funding will reduce the time spent on unsuccessful applications and help to eliminate questionable application practices such as multiple submissions — which are increasingly observed owing tosuccess rates at international funding agencies being as low as 7%. Such a waste of resources has no immediate benefit to the researcher, research progress or society, and there are large opportunity costs in lost research output9.

Because low-risk, low-reward projects are easier to predict and usually implementable in shorter time frames, they lend themselves better to a system in which researchers are evaluated on a project-by-project basis under precarious working conditions and short-term contracts10. Additionally, it is easier to convince reviewers of the benefits of an incremental step instead of potentially ground-breaking projects, which may explain concerns that science is becoming less disruptive. Although a recent initiative by the US National Science Foundation aims to counter this trend by granting individual reviewers the authority to single-handedly fund promising proposals (even if all other reviewers disagree; the so-called golden ticket), this still does not ensure that the aforementioned biases (for example, nepotism and cronyism) are eliminated. On the contrary, the golden ticket could even consolidate already existing and historically grown imbalances by granting individual reviewers even more power to enforce project funding.
