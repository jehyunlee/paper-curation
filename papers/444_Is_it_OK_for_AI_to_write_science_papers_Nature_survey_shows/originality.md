“These results validate what we have also heard from researchers — that there’s great enthusiasm but low adoption of AI to support the research process,” says Josh Jarrett, a sen- ior vice-president at Wiley, the multinational scholarly publisher, which has also surveyed researchers about use of AI.. Split opinions When given the opportunity to comment on their views, researchers’ opinions varied drasti- cally.. On the one hand, some said that the broad adoption of generative AI tools made disclo- sure unnecessary.. “AI will be, if not already is, a norm just like using a calculator,” says Aisawan Petchlorlian, a biomedical researcher at Chula- longkorn University in Bangkok.. “‘Disclosure’ will not be an important issue.” On the other hand, some said that AI use would always be unacceptable.. “I will never con- done using generative AI for writing or review- ing papers, it is pathetic cheating and fraud,” said an Earth-sciences researcher in Canada.. Others were more ambivalent.. Daniel Egan, who studies infectious diseases at the Univer- sity of Cambridge, UK, says that although AI is a time-saver and excellent at synthesizing complex information from multiple sources, relying on it too heavily can feel like cheating oneself.. “By using it, we rob ourselves of the opportunities to learn through engaging with these sometimes laborious processes.” Respondents also raised a variety of con- cerns, from ethical questions around plagia- rism and breaching trust and accountability in the publishing and peer-review process to worries about AI’s environmental impact.. Some said that although they generally accepted that the use of these tools could be ethical, their own experience revealed that AI often produced sub-par results — false citations, inaccurate statements and, as one person described it, “well-formulated crap”.. Respondents also noted that the quality of an AI response could vary widely depending on the specific tool that was used.. There were also some positives: many respondents pointed out that AI could help to level the playing field for academics for whom English was not a first language.. Several also explained why they supported certain uses, but found others unacceptable.. “I use AI to self-translate from Spanish to English and vice versa, complemented with intensive editing of the text, but I would never use AI to generate work from scratch because I enjoy the process of writing, editing and reviewing,” says a humanities researcher from Spain.. “And I would never use AI to review because I would be horrified to be reviewed by AI.” Career stage and location Perhaps surprisingly, academics’ opinions didn’t generally seem to differ widely by their geographical location, research field or career stage.. However, respondents’ self-reported experience with AI for writing or reviewing papers did correlate strongly with having favourable opinions of the scenarios, as might be expected.. Career stage did seem to matter when it came to the most popular use of AI — to edit papers.. Here, younger researchers were both more likely to think the practice acceptable, and more likely to say they had done it (see ‘Impact of career stage for editing with AI’).. Related surveys Various researchers and publishers have conducted surveys of AI use in the academic community, looking broadly at how AI might be used in the scientific process.. In January, Jeremy Ng, a health researcher at the Ottawa Hospital Research Institute in Canada, and his colleagues published a survey of more than 2,000 medical researchers, in which 45% of respondents said they had previously used AI chatbots (J.. Lancet Dig.. Health 7, e94–e102; 2025).. Of those, more than two- thirds said they had used it for writing or editing manuscripts — meaning that, overall, around 31% of the people surveyed had used AI for this purpose.. That is slightly more than in Nature’s survey.. “Our findings revealed enthusiasm, but also hesitation,” Ng says.. “They really reinforced the idea that there’s not a lot of consensus around how, where or for what these chatbots should be used for scientific research.” In February, Wiley published a survey examining AI use in academia by nearly 5,000 researchers around the world (see go.nature.com/438yngu).. Among other find- ings, this revealed that researchers felt most uses of AI (such as writing up documentation and increasing the speed and ease of peer review) would be commonly accepted in the next few years.. But less than half of the respond- ents said they had actually used AI for work, with 40% saying they’d used it for translation and 38% for proofreading or editing of papers.. “The vast majority felt that AI was going to sig- nificantly change their work, both as researcher and author, in the next two years,” Jarrett says.. “But one of the big things holding them back was guidelines in terms of what was safe and ethical use of AI, and what was the appropriate disclosure when that AI use took place.” To disclose or not?. Most research publishers have guidelines on AI that prohibit its use for creating, altering and manipulating data or results, such as images.. They also typically say that an AI tool cannot be listed as an author, because it cannot take responsibility for the work.. But policies vary greatly, especially on disclosure of AI use.. Many publishers now distinguish between different uses of AI.. Often, these are split into two broad categories — improving a piece of text, which includes using AI to enhance spelling, grammar and readability, versus generating new content.. The latter typically requires disclosure, whereas the former does not.. Disclosure is needed, however, when using AI in ways that transcend these basic tasks.. Some journals, such as the US medical publication JAMA, have implemented strict reporting requirements, asking authors who use AI to give details of the software and its manufacturer, as well as a brief description of how it was used.. (This does not apply to the use of these tools in simple tasks such as spell- ing and grammar checking.) Authors are also asked to confirm that they take responsibility for the content that was generated.. “For now, I see it a bit like reporting conflicts of interest — err on the side of disclosure,” says Annette Flanagin, the executive managing edi- tor and vice-president of editorial operations for JAMA in Chicago, Illinois.. Wiley also asks authors to share the specifics of how they used AI.. Some journals, such as Science (published by the American Associa- tion for the Advancement of Science (AAAS) in Washington DC), ask authors to provide prompts as part of the explanation for how they used AI in their work.. Springer Nature, the London-based publisher of Nature, says that generative AI use should be “properly doc- umented”, but that AI-assisted copy-editing need not be declared.. (Nature’s news team is editorially independent of its publisher.) Other publishers have gone in another direction: IOP Publishing in Bristol, UK, for instance, initially required authors to declare AI use, but it has since removed this mandate.. Instead, its policy simply encourages authors to be transparent about their use, emphasizing that authors are responsible for any content that an AI tool creates.. Kim Eggleton, head of peer review and research integrity at IOP Pub- lishing, told Nature in April that this change was made to reflect the ubiquity of these tools.. Publishers are generally less permissive when it comes to using AI for peer review.. Elsevier, in Amsterdam, and the AAAS cur- rently ban reviewers from using generative AI or AI-assisted review, whereas Wiley and Springer Nature permit limited use — with disclosure — and forbid loading material into chatbot websites because of concerns about leaking confidential information.. Respondents to Nature’s survey were also aware of these privacy concerns, with some saying that using AI to assist with peer review was acceptable if done using locally hosted AI that doesn’t leak information online.. Clarity and transparency needed With so much uncertainty about the use of AI and disclosure, the STM published a draft report this April in which it developed a classi- fication of various AI uses in manuscript prepa- ration (see go.nature.com/4tz8bkj).. “We hope that publishers — and the wider ecosystem — will adopt the terminology,” says Joris van Rossum, programme director of STM Solutions, a subsidiary of STM.. Researchers are still in the early days of try- ing to work out the ethics of AI use in academic writing, says Michael Wise, a bioinformaticist and computer scientist at the University of Western Australia in Perth.. Wise is also a trus- tee of the Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE), an advisory group that recommends best practices in scholarly publishing.. “What needs to be emphasized is that the norms of publication ethics have not changed: accurate reporting of what was done and why, and what can be concluded from that, in a con- text of transparency,” he says..
