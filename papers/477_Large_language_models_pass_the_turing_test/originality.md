Abstract We evaluated 4 systems (ELIZA, GPT-4o, LLaMa-3.1-405B, and GPT-4.5) in two randomised, controlled, and pre-registered Turing tests on independent pop- ulations.. Participants had 5 minute conversations simultaneously with another human participant and one of these systems before judging which conversational partner they thought was human.. When prompted to adopt a humanlike persona, GPT-4.5 was judged to be the human 73% of the time: significantly more often than interrogators selected the real human participant.. LLaMa-3.1, with the same prompt, was judged to be the human 56% of the time—not significantly more or less often than the humans they were being compared to—while baseline models (ELIZA and GPT-4o) achieved win rates significantly below chance (23% and 21% respectively).. The results constitute the first empirical evidence that any artificial system passes a standard three-party Turing test..
