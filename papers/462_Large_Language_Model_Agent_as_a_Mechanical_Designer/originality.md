These results highlight the promise of LLM agents to function as a new class of natural language- based, reasoning-driven optimizers capable of independently generating and iteratively improving viable engineering solutions through structured feedback.. Designers must often navigate incom- plete problem definitions, evolving requirements, conflicting objec- tives, and strict time constraints, where nonlinear material behavior and complex coupling between design variables make improvement in one metric (e.g., mass) potentially detrimental to another (e.g., stress) [6–9].. Tasks such as identifying functional architectures, arranging components, optimizing kine- matic behavior, and ensuring manufacturability require designers to reason across multiple abstraction levels and balance qualitative and quantitative trade-offs.. Addressing such broad, heterogeneous, and constraint-rich design spaces remains a major challenge for computational design automation frameworks.. Traditional optimization frameworks, whether gradient-based or gradient-free, often struggle to simultaneously accommodate this diversity of inputs, outputs, and evaluation mechanisms, particularly under dynamic, multi-objective condi- tions where design criteria evolve.. As mechanical systems grow in complexity and the demand for early-stage exploration increases, there is a pressing need for reasoning-driven approaches capable of flexibly integrating diverse information streams, balancing multi- ple objectives, and making coherent, iterative design modifications in response to structured but non-differentiable feedback.. In this study, we evaluate the capa- bility LLMs to autonomously generate optimized truss structures, capturing a fundamental workflow in mechanical engineering: the creation of an initial structural design, evaluation of its perfor- mance, analysis of its behavior under prescribed loading condi- tions, and iterative refinement to satisfy stringent design and func- tional requirements.. To systematically assess the capabilities of LLMs, a framework comprising two primary tasks, each with three specification vari- ations, is established.. The evaluation spans a range of constraint difficulties, enabling a systematic investigation of the model’s abil- ity to follow instructions, satisfy complex design requirements, optimize structural configurations, and reason across successive design iterations.. This framework facilitates the analysis of the LLM’s capacity for solution exploration, iterative refinement, and strategic modifications informed by prior outcomes.. The problem formulation is common to both tasks.. Given a set of predefined support nodes S, load nodes L, and a cross-sectional area dictionary A specifying the available member areas, the LLM must synthesize an optimized truss structure by autonomously: • Generating additional node placements N⊂R2, • Defining the connectivity between nodes as a set of edges E⊂(S∪L∪N) × (S∪L∪N), • Assigning cross-sectional areas Ae∈A to each member e∈ E.. The model must achieve task-specific optimization objectives while adhering to all specified constraints.. Task 1: The LLM must generate a structure that satisfies maximum allowable stress constraints |σe| ≤σmax and a maximum allowable mass constraint Mtotal ≤Mmax.. Once these baseline constraints are satisfied, the model further minimizes the maximum observed stress according to the following objective: min E,Ae,Nmax e∈E|σe| The LLM autonomously determines when to terminate the opti- mization, balancing incremental improvements against diminishing returns from continued refinement.. Task 2: The LLM must design a structure that minimizes the stress-to-weight ratio, defined as: Stress-to-Weight Ratio (swr) = max (|σe|) ∑︁ e∈E(Le× Ae) subject to the constraint Mtotal ≤Mmax.. This task requires the model to navigate complex trade-offs between reducing structural mass and maintaining mechanical robustness.. In both tasks, the LLM is granted the flexibility to add nodes at arbitrary locations, define strategic connections, and select cross- sectional areas from the available set.. Successful design genera- tion depends on effectively balancing exploration of novel struc- tural possibilities with exploitation of established configurations to satisfy performance and constraint requirements.. Navigating this trade-offis essential for producing structurally efficient and com- pliant structures.. Prompt design plays a pivotal role in ef- fectively harnessing the capabilities of LLMs, acting as the critical interface between user intent and model interpretation.. It involves the strategic construction of input queries, or "prompts," to direct the model toward producing outputs that are accurate, relevant, and contextually appropriate [97].. In addition to guiding generation, effective prompt design is essential for extracting targeted informa- tion from the model, ensuring that the responses align closely with the task objectives.. The performance of LLM agents in specialized tasks is highly sensitive to prompt precision and structure; minor variations can substantially impact output quality, consistency, and relevance.. Consequently, prompt engineering has become a funda- mental technique for optimizing LLM-driven workflows, enabling users to steer model behavior reliably and achieve desired outcomes across diverse applications [98, 99].. The design of effective prompts is particularly critical in the context of structural generation and optimization, especially when structured outputs are required for downstream tasks.. Prompt engineering, also referred to as in-context prompting, involves communicating with LLMs to guide their behavior toward desired outcomes without modifying model weights (fine-tuning) [100].. This approach is distinct from prompt design, which focuses on crafting initial inputs that leverage a model’s existing knowledge to optimize performance on specific tasks.. Various methods have been proposed to optimize LLM out- puts through the strategic selection of in-context examples, includ- ing semantic clustering with K-NN [101], constructing directed graphs based on embedding similarity [102], and reinforcement learning techniques such as Q-learning for sample selection [103], among others [104–106].. However, these example selection strategies can introduce biases by favoring samples similar to the training data, which may restrict the model’s ability to explore diverse solutions.. This limitation is particularly problematic in design optimization, where broad exploration is critical for fostering innovation and discovering novel solutions.. To enhance exploration, several methods have been developed.. Self-consistency sampling generates multiple outputs at nonzero temperatures and selects the best candidate [107], while chain-of- thought prompting encourages step-by-step reasoning to improve answer quality [108, 109].. Tree-of-thought prompting extends this by branching reasoning at each step to explore multiple possible outcomes [110].. Although these techniques promote exploration, they can also significantly increase computational cost and out- put complexity.. For instance, self-consistency requires evaluating multiple outputs, and tree-of-thought reasoning can cause an ex- ponential growth in the solution space.. The ReAct framework mitigates some of these challenges by interleaving reasoning traces with task-specific actions, enabling dynamic interaction with external environments and improving the efficiency of solution generation [111].. Our proposed framework leverages the ReAct mechanism [111] to iteratively generate, evaluate, and refine truss structures using a LLM (GPT-4).. Each valid structure undergoes FEM analysis to compute key structural metrics, includ- ing maximum member stresses σeand total mass Mtotal.. These FEM evaluations serve not only as binary constraint checks but also provide detailed quantitative feedback, measuring how closely the generated structure meets stress and mass requirements.. All generated structures and their associated FEM results are stored in a growing history set H, which captures feasible designs, partially feasible solutions, and failed attempts.. A ranked history R is maintained, prioritizing feasible designs based on minimum maximum stress and ordering infeasible designs by proximity to constraint satisfaction.. This structured memory allows the LLM to reason over previous iterations, leveraging patterns of success, incremental improvements, and common failure modes to guide future generations.. At each iteration, the LLM is provided with the current de- sign, the complete design history, and the iteration count (up to a maximum of 25 iterations).. Based on the outcome of the FEM evaluation, an adaptive prompting strategy is employed: if the generated structure satisfies all constraints, an optimiza- tion prompt (msg_optim) directs the model to further minimize stress or improve structural efficiency; if the structure is infea- sible, a constraint-correction prompt (msg_constraint) instructs the LLM to analyze constraint violations and propose modifica- tions.. Throughout the process, the LLM leverages the FEM results and ranked history of prior designs to identify favorable patterns, correct past mistakes, and guide new generations.. The model is explicitly tasked with autonomously terminating the optimization process once no further meaningful improvements are achievable.. For Task 2, the optimization strategy is staged: initially focus- ing on satisfying the constraint Mtotal ≤Mmax and swr≤swrmax, followed by minimizing the stress-to-weight ratio.. This sequential focus ensures that both critical performance objectives are system- atically addressed.. Traditional structural optimization methods, such as Bayesian optimization, genetic algorithms, and ground structure methods (GSM), have been widely used for continuous parame- ter tuning and topology optimization.. However, these approaches struggle with highly discrete, multi-faceted design spaces charac- terized by continuous nodal positions and discrete member con- nections and area ID assignments.. In contrast, LLMs represent a new class of optimizers that integrate generation and optimization through flexible, context-driven reasoning.. Bayesian optimization is effective for continuous, low- dimensional problems but struggles with discrete, structured generation tasks such as adding nodes and modifying graph 9 topologies.. Our attempts to adapt Bayesian optimization to this problem were unsuccessful due to the difficulty of handling categorical selection and dynamic graph growth.. Ground structure methods (GSM) optimize trusses by selecting connections between fixed nodes and assuming continuous mem- ber sizing.. GSM is poorly suited for problems requiring new node generation and discrete area ID selection, and our GSM-based at- tempts did not yield meaningful solutions due to the combinatorial complexity introduced by these factors.. As a conventional optimization benchmark, we employed the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA II) [112] us- ing the pymoo framework [113].. NSGA II is a widely used evo- lutionary algorithm known for its ability to handle multiobjective optimization problems involving mixed variable types while main- taining diversity across the Pareto front.. However, it faces notable limitations, including premature convergence, limited structural reasoning, and high computational cost, particularly in discrete and combinatorially large design spaces such as truss generation with dynamic topology.. For benchmarking, NSGA II was initialized with a fixed num- ber of nodes and a constraint on the maximum allowable num- ber of member connections.. The algorithm simultaneously opti- mized nodal coordinates, selected member connections, and as- signed cross-sectional area IDs from a predefined discrete set.. Nodal positions were bounded within a rectangular domain span- ning (0, -5) to (10, 5) to maintain both realism and flexibility.. When these bounds were removed—specifically allowing the algorithm to determine the number of nodes and connections—it consistently failed to generate feasible or meaningful truss configurations, even after 1000 generations with a population size of 100 (amounting to 100,000 FEM evaluations).. Although our FEM evaluator is com- putationally efficient, the brute force nature of evolutionary search becomes impractical for highly discrete and complex design spaces without well-constrained problem settings.. This illustrates NSGA II’s dependence on carefully defined boundaries to perform effec- tive optimization.. To ensure a fair comparison, the number of nodes and members used in NSGA II runs was aligned with the statistical distribution observed in structures generated by the LLM (Appendix E).. Each run was executed for 500 generations with a population size of 100 to balance solution quality and computational effort across both methods.. In contrast, LLMs dynamically propose layouts, introduce new nodes, select area IDs from discrete sets, and balance objectives like mass, stress, and feasibility through reasoning rather than gradient-based search or surrogate modeling.. This integrated gen- eration and optimization capability enables LLMs to solve com- plex, highly structured problems that traditional methods often struggle to address.. Thus, reasoning-based optimization through LLMs offers a promising alternative paradigm for complex struc- tural design challenges, combining topological creativity with rig- orous constraint management.. LLMs introduce a new class of optimization agents capable of tackling structural design tasks in ways that differ fundamentally from conventional methods such as gradient-based solvers, evolutionary algorithms, and even learning- based strategies like meta-learning, reinforcement learning (RL), and no-regret optimization.. Their unique advantages become espe- cially apparent in design problems characterized by mixed-variable types, large combinatorial design spaces, and black-box evaluation models where gradient information is unavailable.. LLMs are particularly well suited for mixed-variable optimiza- tion problems like truss design, where continuous parameters (e.g., nodal positions) must be optimized alongside discrete choices (e.g., connectivity graphs and member areas).. Traditional methods often require careful encoding of discrete decisions and separate treat- ment for continuous and categorical variables.. In contrast, LLMs naturally operate over both types through prompt-based reasoning, generating coherent design candidates without hard separation of variable types.. In black-box scenarios, where evaluation relies solely on external solvers like FEM without access to analytical gradients or internal reward structure, LLMs demonstrate a key advantage.. While rein- forcement learning or no-regret algorithms can, in theory, learn from such environments, they typically require extensive inter- action, reward engineering, or simulator-specific tuning.. Meta- learning approaches, on the other hand, often depend on task dis- tributions and curated training procedures that may not generalize well to novel or ill-posed design tasks.. LLMs bypass these re- quirements by leveraging natural language reasoning, few-shot in- context learning, and structured feedback loops to iteratively refine their outputs with minimal setup.. Compared to RL-based or no-regret approaches, LLMs offer a more interpretable and user-steerable framework.. Designers can ar- ticulate goals, constraints, or corrections in natural language, which LLMs interpret directly—eliminating the need for hand-crafted re- ward functions or policy tuning.. This supports a more human- centric, co-design workflow, where iterative reasoning, memory of past failures, and symbolic understanding help navigate complex, non-convex design spaces.. Moreover, LLMs implicitly learn feasibility patterns from their training data and adapt over successive prompts, often producing structurally valid designs without requiring explicitly encoded con- straints.. This contrasts with traditional optimizers and RL agents, which may generate physically invalid configurations unless penal- ized or constrained through engineered loss terms.. However, LLMs do have limitations.. They struggle with floating-point precision, particularly for fine-tuning continuous pa- rameters, since token-based generation lacks the granularity and optimization mechanisms available in numerical solvers.. Addi- tionally, in the absence of explicit objective functions or reward signals, LLMs may converge slowly or terminate prematurely if not guided with carefully constructed prompts and structured feed- back.. Their generative reasoning is powerful, but not inherently optimized for numerical convergence.. In summary, LLMs are best positioned not as replacements for traditional or learning-based optimizers, but as a new class of reasoning-based design agents.. Their strengths lie in early-stage, ill-posed, or combinatorially rich problems where the design space is vast, the objective is ambiguous, and structured reasoning and symbolic flexibility are more valuable than pure numerical preci- sion.. Their ability to operate in black-box settings, handle diverse variable types, incorporate feedback, and communicate in natural language marks a significant shift in the applicability of AI for structural and mechanical design.. 5 Conclusion In conclusion, the use of LLM agents as optimizers in engineer- ing design marks a transformative shift in how design solutions are conceived and refined.. LLMs excel in parsing natural lan- guage inputs and, through in-context learning, can iteratively adapt to meet precise specifications without the need for training for a specific task.. This capability is enhanced by their adeptness at rea- soning through and exploring a wide range of design alternatives while also focusing on the most promising solutions.. By integrat- ing LLMs with the finite element method (FEM), the framework provides a robust means of assessing and improving design out- comes.. This continuous iterative process, fueled by the feedback loop between the LLM and FEM, ensures that each design itera- tion moves closer to the ideal specification..
