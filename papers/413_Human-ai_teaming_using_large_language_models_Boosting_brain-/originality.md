ABSTRACT Recently, there is an increasing interest in using artificial intelligence (AI) to automate aspects of the research process, or even autonomously conduct the full research cycle from idea generation, over data analysis, to composing and evaluation of scientific manuscripts.. Examples of working “AI scientist” systems have been demonstrated for computer science tasks and running molecular biology labs.. While some approaches aim for full autonomy of the scientific AI, others rather aim for leveraging human-AI teaming.. Here, we address how to adapt such approaches for boosting Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) development, as well as brain research resp.. neuroscience at large.. We argue that at this time, a strong emphasis on human-AI teaming, in contrast to fully autonomous “AI BCI researcher,” will be the most promising way forward..
