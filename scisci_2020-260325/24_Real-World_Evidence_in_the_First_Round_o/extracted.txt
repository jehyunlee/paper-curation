 Abstract:  
Objectives : To quantify and characterize the use of Real World Evidence (RWE) in the Centers 
for Medicare and Medicaid ’s (CMS) in CMS drug pricing negotiations to assess the transparency 
and methodological consistency of RWE incorporation.  
Methods:  We extracted and reviewed all 1,822 unique citations across the ten MFP Explanation 
documents available on CMS ’s website. For each citation, we identified whether the study 
constitu ted RWE, the data source (e.g., claims, electronic health records registry/clinical cohort), 
study design, and whether external health technology assessments were cited. We calculated 
drug-specific and overall RWE proportions and summarized the methodologi cal features of RWE 
studies.  
Results: RWE was cited for all ten drugs, but its use varied substantially. The share of RWE 
ranged from 13.8% (Farxiga) to 41.5% (Xarelto). Across all drugs, nearly half of RWE studies 
used claims/administrative data (47.0%), followed by EHR data (17.1%) and registry/clinical 
cohort data (17.1%). Most RWE studies employed cohort or longitudinal designs (82.1%). Three 
drugs ’ citation lists included external HTAs (ICER or NICE). Despite broad inclusion of RWE, 
the MFP documents d id not specify how RWE influenced negotiated prices or how CMS 
evaluated the quality or relevance of these studies.  
Conclusion: These findings underscore the need for greater transparency and consistent 
methodological standards for evaluating RWE, aligning  Medicare ’s process more closely with 
international health technology assessment practices.

 Highlights:  
• This study systematically examines the evidence base  behind Medicare ’s first IRA drug 
price negotiations, clarifying how real -world evidence (RWE) was identified, classified, 
and used, quantifying its application for the first time, despite prior calls for greater RWE 
integration.

therapeutic areas.

weighting RWE in Medicare price setting, with important implications for HEOR 
practice, evidence development strategies, and the long -term credibility of IRA 
negotiations.

 Introduction  
 
The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) was enacted in 2022 in response to concerns over escalating 
prescription drug costs, explicitly allowing Medicare to negotiate prices for certain high -cost 
medications covered under Medicare Part D (1).   
 
Despite their high costs , Medicare could not historically  negotiate drug prices directly due to the 
“non-interference clause ” embedded in  the 2003 Medicare Modernization Act  (6). Instead, 
pricing determinations  were left to private negotiations between insurance plan sponsors, 
pharmacy benefit managers, and manufacturers  (7, 8) . The IRA changed this  framework  by 
granting Medicare the explicit authority to negotiate prices. In 2025, CMS held negotiations for 
the first 10 drugs (chosen due to their high cost and high volume)  (10). Although CMS is 
authorized to consider comparative effectiveness and therapeutic alternatives, including real -
world evidence (RWE), there has been no systematic characterization of how RWE is cited or 
categorized within the publicly av ailable Maximum Fair Price (MFP) Explanation documents. 
Existing commentary has largely focused on the policy implications of negotiation authority 
rather than the empirical documentation of evidence use.  
 
Given this new Medicare negotiation authority, the  methods and evidence used to determine 
appropriate pricing become critically important. RWE represents a valuable form of evidence 
that should be considered. RWE is clinical evidence that is generated using real -world data 
(RWD), including electronic heal th records  (EHR) , insurance claims, patient registries, and

 wearable devices  (11). While randomized control trials ( RCTs ) remain  the gold standard for 
assessing efficacy  in controlled environments , they have limitations that constrain their 
applicability  to price negotiations. For example, RCTs use strict inclusion criteria, which creates a 
homogenous study population that may not reflect the demographics and clinical diversity of 
actual populations  (12). RWE addresses these limitations by capturing effectiveness in routine 
clinical practice, providing a more complete picture of outcomes, patient experiences, and long -
term value  (11).  
 
Under the IRA , CMS is authorized (but not required) to consider comparative effectiveness and 
therapeutic alternatives in studies including RWE. Therefore, this study conducts a structured 
citation analysis of the first ten MFP Explanation documents to quantify the pre valence, 
sources, and study designs of cited RWE. In this context, “use” refers to citation within publicly 
available documents and does not imply evidentiary weighting or influence on final negotiated 
prices.  
 
Data Source and Methods  
Data for this study w ere gathered from  the MFP Explanation documents available on CMS ’s 
websites for the 10 negotiated drugs  (13). For each explanation document, one investigator 
assessed all the references , and compiled a  comprehensive  list of all referenced citations, noting 
the drug,  the study assessed, if it included RWE, the data source, the type of study (i.e., 
systematic review, retrospective cohort study, etc), and the study outcome. The results were 
checked and reviewed by the second investigator. Any discrepancies were resolved  by

 consensus or a third investigator. In this analysis , all the  references of  real-world clinical settings, 
used to inform healthcare decision -making , was classifi ed as RWE , This definition was very 
inclusive covering  not only real-world  databases  studies (such as EHR / claims  / administrative 
databases , etc.), registry/clinical cohort stud ies and  survey/questionnaire studies  were included, 
but also systematic reviews/meta -analyses of real-world  studies , case control studies  and some 
descriptive st udies.   
 
With this information, the researchers calculated the proportion of studies for each drug that 
included RWE, as well as the presence of more comprehensive assessments  (e.g., ICER 
assessment) . Ultimately, an overall proportion  of RWE  among all cita tions  for all 10 drugs was 
determined.  For purposes of this analysis, “use” of RWE was operationalized as citation within 
the publicly available MFP Explanation documents. The analysis does not assess the relative 
weighting of cited studies, the deliberati ve process underlying negotiations, or the influence of 
any individual study on final price determinations, as such information is not publicly disclosed.  
 
Results  
 
A total of 1,824 references were reviewed. After removing two duplicates, there were 1,822 
references cited in the MFP explanation documents for all 10 negotiated drugs.  Of these 1,822 
citations, 502 (27.6%) were classified as RWE.  These counts reflect citations within publicly 
available MFP Explanation documents and do not represent the full se t of materials reviewed 
during negotiations.

Exhibit 1 presents the total number of studies reviewed and the proportion classified as RWE for 
each drug. The volume of citations varied substantially, ranging from 94 (Entresto) to 283 
(Eliquis). The propor tion of RWE citations also differed meaningfully across drugs, from 13.8% 
(Farxiga) to 41.5% (Xarelto). Three drugs —Farxiga, Imbruvica, and Jardiance —had less than 20% 
RWE representation. In contrast, Eliquis and Xarelto exceeded 30%, with Xarelto demonstr ating 
the highest proportion overall. Most remaining drugs clustered between 20% and 30%.  
 
As shown in Exhibit 2 , the most frequently used data source in RWE studies is 
claims/administrative databases, accounting for 47.01%, followed by EHR databases and 
registry/clinical cohort study data, each accounting for 17.13%. Some studies also utilized 
survey/questionnaire data (8.17%). Other data sources include systematic reviews / meta -
analyses of RWE studies based on literature databases  (3.59%) , self-collected  data gathered by 
investigators (1.99%), government databases ( e.g. FDA FEARS and CDC WONDER databases)  
(1.20%) , or mixed source  or not specified source  (3.78%) .  Exhibit  3 shows the types of all RWE 
studies. The vast majority employed cohort, or longitudinal designs (82.07%), followed by cross -
sectional studies or surveys (10.96%), while 3.59% were systematic reviews or meta -analyses of 
RWS.  Other designs included case -control, time -series, and descriptive economic analyses.  
 
Three documents cited  external health technology assessments, including ICER reports (Eliquis, 
Xarelto, Enbrel) and a NICE appraisal (Farxiga).

 Discussion  
This study descriptively characterizes RWE citations within the initial round of IRA drug price 
negotiations and finds that 27.6% of cited studies were classified as RWE, with substantial 
variability across drugs (13.8% –41.5%) and a predominance of claims -based data sources (47%). 
While RWE was cited within publicly available MFP Explanation documents , the relative 
weighting of that evidence in negotiations is not publicly disclosed . Furthermore, the specifics 
of the process for exchanging revised offers an d counteroffers were confidential and not 
included in the MFP Explanation document s. Consequently, we remain unaware of whether or 
how RWE impacted IRA decisions.  The observed variability in RWE citation across drugs may 
reflect differences in therapeutic area maturity, the availability of published RWE , or 
submission patterns rather than differences in evidentiary valuation. Because CMS does not 
disclose how evide nce types are weighted in negotiation deliberations, this study provides a 
descriptive account of documented citations rather than an evaluation of decision -making 
influence.  
 
This list does not represent the totality of evidence that CMS reviewed during t he negotiation 
process. CMS did not use evidence from comparative clinical effectiveness research in a manner 
that treats extending the life of an individual who is elderly, disabled, or terminally ill as of lower 
value than extending the life of an indivi dual who is younger, nondisabled, or not terminally ill, 
and did not use quality -adjusted  life years.

 The IRA grants  CMS  broad discretion to establish the MFP using various factors , including the 
extent to which a drug represents a therapeutic advancement , comparative effectiveness 
across clinical outcomes, and if a drug addresses unmet medical needs. CMS may also consider 
evidence related to therapeutic alternatives, research and development costs, and production 
and distribution expenses in developing pr icing  (13). However, this framework lacks specificity 
regarding evidence  evaluation standards and weighting methodologies.  
 
Critical gaps remain in understanding how CMS evaluates and prioritizes RWE  compared to 
RCTs or other data, and where there are consistent standards applied to all drugs.  As noted , it is 
unclear whether r elevant evidence was not available, not submitted, or not cited, nor is it 
understood how CMS evaluated different types of evidence . Limited disclosure regarding 
evidentiary weighting constrains external assessment of how different forms of evidence 
contri bute to final price determinations.  Stakeholders , including patients and policymakers, 
cannot determine where the MFP reflects genuine,  real-world therapeutic  value. Additionally , 
the opacity prevents stakeholders from assessing the rigor and fairness of pricing decisions and 
the role of sponsors. Lastly , inconsistent application of evidence  may create non -transparent 
variations in pricing determinations. These documentation gaps may limit the ability of external 
stakeholders to assess process consistency and identify opportunities for refinement. In the 
context of a politically sensitive policy such as drug pricing reform, limited transparency may 
contribute to stakeholder uncerta inty and increase scrutiny of the negotiation framework (14).

 Although this study does not evaluate evidentiary weighting or internal deliberations, the 
docum ented variability in RWE citation (ranging from 13.8% to 41.5%) and the predominance of 
claims -based studies (47%) suggest opportunities to improve clarity in reporting and 
documentation practices. First, CMS could provide greater clarity within MFP Explan ation 
documents regarding how different categories of evidence, including RWE, are evaluated. 
Second, CMS should establish explicit quality standards for RWE, emphasizing data provenance, 
methodological rigor, bias reduction strategies, and comprehensive d ocumentation 
requirements.  These standards would help ensure that evidence is not only included  but also 
used consistently.  This is especially important as RWE including claims databases offer broad 
population coverage and longitudinal follow -up which may limit contextual interpretation 
relative to electronic health record –based analyses. Third , CMS should encourage and requir e 
external submissions of RWE from independent researchers, health systems, and patient 
advocacy groups. While this occurs on a limited scale currently, creating more transparent and 
inclusive processes could broaden evidence bases and reduce dependence on  manufacturer -
provided data, thereby strengthening the credibility of price -setting decisions.  
 
Established international  and U.S.-based  models demonstrate the feasibility of these sorts of 
policies. For example, the United Kingdom’s National Institute fo r Health and Care Excellence 
has developed systematic, transparent, and reproducible methodological frameworks for 
incorporating RWE into health technology assessments , with structured consultation periods 
allowing stakeholder organizations to comment on d raft guidelines  (15, 16) . Similarly, the 
Institute for Clinical and Economic Review , a U.S. -based non -governmental agency producing

 evaluations on drugs, establishes value assessment frameworks that guide the development of 
comprehensive  evidence reports  (17). These practices show that transparency and 
methodological rigor are possible during drug price -setting.  
 
These observations highlight that, beyond price reductions, clarity regarding evidentiary 
documentation may influence how stakeholders interpret the negotiation framework. Greater 
methodological transparency could help external audiences better understand how negotiated 
prices relate to documented evidence. In contrast, adopting a transparent framework with 
clearly defined RWE standards and inclusive evidence submissions would affirm CMS’s 
commitment to evidence -based decision -making. This approach would bo lster stakeholder trust 
and ensure that negotiated prices accurately reflect true comparative value. Such reforms may 
strengthen the perceived legitimacy and durability of the IRA as documentation practices 
evolve.  
 
Conclusion  
This study provides, to our k nowledge, the first descriptive characterization of RWE citations 
within the first Medicare price negotiation process. Although RWE was cited across all MFP 
explanation documents, its prevalence and sources varied and the evidentiary weighting 
remains undi sclosed. As such, this study was not able to identify policy impact from the patterns 
of RWE citations. Greater clarity in documentation practices and the negotiation process could 
improve understanding of how evidence is incorporated in pricing decisions and guide future 
research and policy.
