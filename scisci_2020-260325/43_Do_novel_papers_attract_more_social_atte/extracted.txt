A B S T R A C T
The proliferation of social media and the increasing emphasis on societal impact on research 
evaluation have highlighted the importance of understanding how scientific novelty influences 
public engagement. This study examines how the novelty of scientific research correlates with the 
social attention it garners. The analysis is based on a dataset comprising over 310,000 articles in 
economics and business published from 2012 to 2019. We use Altmetrics—comprising the 
Altmetric Attention Score, Mendeley, Twitter, Blogs, News, Facebook, and Wikipedia—to 
evaluate the social attention received by both novel and non-novel papers as defined by us. The 
results show that novel research consistently garners greater social attention, with particularly 
strong effects observed in top-tier journals and open-access publications. Our findings remain 
robust even when employing alternative measures of novelty and controlling for the citation 
count. Further analysis comparing outcomes across various Altmetric sources provides a 
comprehensive view of how different platforms reflect societal engagement with novel research. 
This study provides valuable insights into the societal attention of novel and original scientific 
research, offering a new perspective for academic publishing and research evaluation.
1. Introduction
In recent years, the proliferation of social media has fundamentally transformed the landscape of scientific communication, 
extending its reach beyond traditional academic audiences to engage a more diverse public. Within the academic community, there has 
been an increasing emphasis from governments and public funding agencies on the necessity for scientists to demonstrate the societal 
impact of their research endeavors (Bornmann, 2013; Salter et al., 2017; Ou et al., 2024). In line with policy demands that extend 
beyond traditional evaluation models based on citation and productivity (Waltman, 2016), public attention has become a critical 
factor in assessing the societal impact of scientific research (Díaz-Faes et al., 2019; Robinson-Garcia et al., 2018). This also underscores 
the importance of understanding the factors that drive public interest in scientific inquiry.
Research on unprecedented events, especially large-scale epidemic events, is typically considered novel. The COVID-19 pandemic 
served as a typical observational window, during which research papers related to the pandemic topics experienced a rapid accu­
mulation of academic citations and social media mentions, significantly outpacing other subjects (Kousha & Thelwall, 2020). Although 
previous studies have shown a weak or negative correlation between Twitter mentions and academic impact (Costas et al., 2015; 
Haustein et al., 2014; Thelwall et al., 2013), this research found that, in the early stages of COVID-19 research dissemination, Twitter 
mentions became an effective early indicator of academic impact (Kousha & Thelwall, 2020). This study highlights how urgent and

Existing empirical studies have systematically discussed the relationship between novelty and academic citations, and there is a 
threshold in the positive impact of novelty on citation counts. (Yan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021). While interdisciplinary research, 
which is related to and can promote novelty in some way, enhances social visibility for scientists (D’Este & Robinson-García, 2023), the 
two concepts are not always the same. Therefore, the mechanism by which novelty in research papers affects social attention remains 
under-explored. More specifically, it is unclear whether research with greater novelty is more likely to attract public interest on 
platforms such as social media, thus facilitating broader public dissemination and engagement.
This study investigates the impact of novelty in scientific research on social attention by analyzing a sample of over 380,000 ac­
ademic journal articles from the fields of economics and business. Using altmetrics, such as social media usage counts, we quantita­
tively assess social attention, drawing on prior work (D’Este & Robinson-García, 2023; Robinson-Garcia et al., 2018; Wouters et al., 
2019). Our analysis explores the positive effects of research novelty on public engagement and also considers factors that may in­
fluence the consistency of this relationship. This study helps our understanding of how the novelty of scientific research boosts social 
visibility, enhances public communication, and ultimately bridges the gap between science and society, motivating research with 
broader societal impact.
This paper is organized as follows. The following section presents the relevant literature. Section 3 presents the data and methods 
used in the study. Section 4 contains the data analysis, including descriptive analysis, benchmark model analysis, robustness analysis, 
heterogeneous analysis, and additional analysis. Section 5 engages in discussion and a summary of the major conclusions.
2. Literature review
2.1. Research on novelty
In the tradition of economic management studies, novelty is the result of recombining what is already known “differently” 
(Schumpeter & Backhaus, 2003). Technology does not emerge from a void but is derived from existing knowledge (Ozman, 2012). 
Recent research has supported this perspective in which scientific novelty is identified by remote (Wang et al., 2017) and atypical (Uzzi 
et al., 2013) combinations of the backward citations. Indicators about knowledge sources that have been proposed in prior research 
include references, cited journals, and knowledge entities within papers like keywords (Dahlin & Behrens, 2005; Lee et al., 2015; 
Trapido, 2015; Uddin et al., 2015; Uzzi et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2017, 2018).
Dahlin & Behrens (2005) were among the first to propose the use of paired references as a metric for novelty, asserting that this 
criterion can identify potential radical inventions before market introduction. This method was later adopted by Trapido (2015), who 
discussed the Matthew effect of novelty in scientific publications. Despite its ability to identify more complex and unconventional 
combinations of existing knowledge, it is less frequently used due to its high computational demands (Matsumoto et al., 2021). Uddin 
et al. (2015) introduced a framework for exploring knowledge structures through keyword combinations to examine emerging fields in 
multidisciplinary research. Uzzi et al. (2013) investigated the combination atypicality of journal pairs cited in publications. Bornmann 
et al. (2019a) validated the novelty indicators proposed by Uzzi et al. (2013) and Wang et al. (2017) using peer review data from 
biomedical research and concluded that only the indicator developed by Uzzi et al. (2013) effectively reflects peer review assessments.
Some studies have pointed out that the methods for measuring the scientific novelty of papers are related to the ways of quantifying 
interdisciplinary research (Fontana et al., 2020), and the interdisciplinary characteristics of a paper can promote its novelty (D’Este 
et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024). However, it is noteworthy that interdisciplinarity and novelty do not always capture the same aspects of 
research outcomes. For instance, Nobel Prize-winning research, which is often considered novel, is not always interdisciplinary (Szell 
et al., 2018). Another article highlighted the negative correlation between the CD index and the potential for papers to connect with 
society (Yang et al., 2024). There is a research gap in the relationship between the novelty of studies and social attention.
The impact of novel works is also inconsistent and often subject to contrasting viewpoints. The first perspective supports that the 
novelty and uniqueness of a paper signify an important integration of old and new ideas, generating substantial new knowledge and 
garnering widespread recognition (Foster et al., 2015). A comprehensive assessment of the overall value of a paper should include 
consideration of both impact and novelty (Yan & Fan, 2024). Such novelty increases the likelihood of making a significant impact on 
other papers (Criscuolo et al., 2013) and can serve as a key driver of radical technological innovation (Ozman, 2012). Furthermore, the 
premium associated with novelty can be transferred from the authors’ identity to their work, and also inherited from mentors to the 
work of their mentees (Trapido, 2015). On the other hand, excessively novel research may face resistance in certain respects. For 
example, as novel components increase in a work, so does the quantity of original knowledge elements, which can complicate un­
derstanding (Verhoeven et al., 2016). There may be a risk of insufficient insight into the foundational knowledge or an inability to 
recognize potential weaknesses in their research (Kaplan & Vakili, 2015). The combination of broad concepts may be too difficult to 
interpret and may even hinder reviewers and readers from fully appreciating the value of the research (Yan et al., 2020).
2.2. Research on social attention and altmetrics
The benefits that society derives from scientific progress can only be realized when scientific outputs achieve full exposure to the 
social audience (Bornmann, 2013). As such, the societal visibility of scientific production and its value has become a crucial metric for 
assessing its impact. The limitations of traditional bibliometric methods in capturing the broader societal influence, such as 
commercialization or teaching of research, have spurred growing interest in alternative indicators, particularly altmetrics, which serve

to complement traditional citation-count-tracking measures (Mohammadi & Thelwall, 2014; Priem, 2015; Priem et al., 2011; Lemke 
et al., 2022). Altmetrics, or more specifically social media metrics, were initially developed to gauge the online engagement with 
scientific work. Over time, with the involvement of commercial data providers (major Altmetric aggregators like Altmetric.com, 
Lagotto, Plum Analytics, and CrossRef Event Data), the scope of Altmetric sources and the diversity of the indicators themselves have 
expanded considerably (Haustein, 2016; Robinson-García et al., 2014; Zahedi & Costas, 2018). Hence, Altmetrics offers methodo­
logical advantages in terms of broadness, diversity, speed, and openness (Wouters & Costas, 2012).
A growing body of literature has argued that Altmetrics have established a foundation for indexing the societal impact of research 
beyond traditional academic spheres (Bornmann et al., 2019b; Mohammadi & Thelwall, 2014). Bornmann et al. (2019a) employed a 
subset of Altmetric data in conjunction with the UK REF dataset to scrutinize the convergent validity of altmetrics and revealed that 
Altmetric scores are often nearly uncorrelated with expert reviewer scores. However, this discrepancy suggests that altmetrics might 
capture a unique form of societal impact—one potentially overlooked by academic reviewers—that includes dimensions of public 
engagement and socially relevant practical applications. Consequently, the use of altmetrics as a measure of societal attention in this 
study is both theoretically and empirically justified.
However, Robinson-Garcia et al. (2018) argued that the capacity of altmetrics to reflect social impact is deeply intertwined with the 
selection of specific indicators and the conceptual dimensions they represent. Zhang et al. (2019) demonstrated that, among all 
Altmetric sub-indicators, the number of Mendeley readers is the most effective metric for distinguishing highly cited publications from 
those experiencing rapid citation growth. Bornmann & Haunschild (2018) found that Mendeley readership and citations converge on a 
single factor significantly associated with research quality, as measured by F1000Prime. In contrast, Twitter activity loads onto a 
separate factor that is unrelated to research quality. There is a lack of research on whether different social media sources’ 
sub-indicators vary in their ability to reflect the novelty attributes of articles, and we will further analyze this in a later section.
Since the 21st century, the dynamics of open access have been of significant concern and have received substantial support from the 
scientific community (Lawrence, 2001; Maddi & Sapinho, 2022). Apart from OACA (Open Access Citation Advantage) (Eysenbach, 
2006; Norris et al., 2008), some researchers claim that open access (OA) can facilitate the flow of scientific works to the public, thereby 
benefiting society by reducing efficiency losses in the innovation process (Gold, 2021; Nelson & Eggett, 2017). This partially explains 
the intrinsic mechanisms by which novelty influences societal attention. Research has also demonstrated that top-tier journals are 
more likely to attract a larger and more engaged readership, reducing scholars’ reliance on search engines to access papers published in 
these journals (Ma et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022).
This study examines papers from economics and business journals to explore the relationship between novelty in scientific pub­
lications and the social attention they receive. The findings offer insights into the topic of ‘How and to what extent scientific findings’ 
novelty contribute to their social attention by boosting social engagement and public involvement on social media platforms’. The 
following questions should be considered deeply: 
RQ1. Do papers with high-level novelty receive more social attention when controlling for confounding factors?
RQ2. How does novelty relate to the social impact and attention of papers when considering the heterogeneity across open access 
status and top-tier journals?
RQ3. What differences are observed when further examining social attention across different media?
3. Data and method
3.1. Data collection
The primary dataset for this study was derived from the Microsoft Academic Graph (MAG) and the SciSciNet database (Lin et al., 
2023). The SciSciNet database is a large-scale open data lake for scientific research, encompassing >134 million scientific publications. 
The proposed data lake enhances the robustness and reproducibility of research by minimizing redundant work in data processing and 
measurement (Lin et al., 2023).
We retrieved all published journal articles in economics and business from MAG and limited the sample to those published between 
2012 and 2019. The distribution of the articles by year is shown in Fig. 1. The data were truncated in 2019 to ensure that each article 
had at least a three-year time window, thereby guaranteeing that the articles received sufficient attention. Additionally, since Alt­
metric.com was established in 2011, the data were confined to the period of 2012 to 2019.
To refine our dataset, we excluded all documents that did not provide a Digital Object Identifier (DOI) in MAG, as we required the 
DOI for each article to retrieve the altmetrics scores via the Altmetric API. We selected seven metrics, including Altmetric Attention 
Score, Mendeley, News, Twitter, Facebook, Wikipedia, Blogs. It should be noted that all altmetrics are replaced with values of 0 if an 
article has no records on Altmetric.com. We also removed articles from our analysis sample for which the novelty variables measured 
by uncommon bibliography combinations could not be obtained from the SciSciNet database, since some often lack proper references 
focusing on evaluating and reporting new research like the MIT Sloan Management Review. The final dataset comprises 316,303 
papers.

of citation distributions, a Z-score is generated for each journal-pair. A lower Z-score indicates that the combination appears to be 
innovative, as the journal-pair is relatively rare within the field.

3.2.1. Social attention
In the main analysis, we used the Altmetric Attention Score data obtained from Altmetric.com to quantify social attention. To 
address the skewness in the distribution, we applied a natural logarithmic transformation with an additive constant.
Fig. 2 presents the distribution of the Altmetric attention scores and six sub-metrics collected from Altmetric.com, categorized by 
whether the papers are classified as "novel-papers" within our sample. Across each metric, papers identified as “novel paper” 
demonstrate a notable relative advantage. Specifically, the mean social attention garnered by novel-papers is approximately two to 
three times higher than that of non-novel papers. These findings provide preliminary evidence for the subsequent analysis of the 
relationship between a paper’s novelty and its social attention.

To formally examine the relationship between the novelty of academic papers and the social attention received, we constructed the 
following benchmark model-a linear regression model with fixed effects:

4.3. Robustness checks
In this section, we check the robustness of the regression to provide further evidence of the validity of the current effect by changing 
variables, using alternative measurements, reducing the sample size, and focusing on specific research areas. First, to further 
demonstrate the impact of an article’s novelty on the social attention it subsequently receives, we changed the counting method of 
independent variables by using binary variables instead of counting variables. Papers with a 10pct-zscore <0 are classified as novel- 
papers and assigned a value of 1; all other papers are assigned a value of 0 (Yang et al., 2022). The regression results remain stable 
using “novel-paper” as the independent variable, as shown in the first column of Table 5. According to the results, when a paper 
changes from being non-novel to novel, the Altmetric score increases by approximately 10 %.
Second, alternative metrics to quantify novelty are employed. On the one hand, we adopted a more recent metric—the disruption 
index (Funk & Owen-Smith, 2017; Wu et al., 2019), which quantifies scientific novelty by measuring the extent to which a paper 
disrupts or substitutes the existing knowledge by its citation patterns (Liang et al., 2023). On the other hand, we applied the textual 
novelty measurement established by Kelly et al. (2021), defining academic novelty as research work that demonstrates conceptual 
distinctiveness from its predecessors while exhibiting reduced reliance on existing scholarly contributions. We implemented their 
high-dimensional NLP framework across our samples’ titles and abstracts (2012–2014 corpus, n = 110,566). The results in Columns 2 
and 3 of Table 5 validate that our conclusions remain consistent across different measurement frameworks, and these multi-metric 
approaches addresses potential bias from any single operationalization.
Third, we filtered out papers with no Altmetric score and excluded those with novelty scores exceeding the 95th percentile and 
below the 5th percentile to reduce the impact of outliers. We applied a similar procedure, and the results confirmed its significance, as 
evidenced in Columns 4 and 5 of Table 5. Forth, we conducted regression analyses separately on the samples from the two selected 
journal fields (business & economics) to ensure consistency and robustness. As depicted in Columns 6 and 7 of Table 5, the primary 
observed coefficients stand at 0.035 and 0.024 and hold statistical significance at the 0.01 level, in line with the findings of the baseline 
regression model. Finally, we included “Citation” as a control variable to mitigate the potential influence of quality (as Column 8 of 
Table 5), and the results remain significant. We still obtained the citation counts for articles from MAG and applied a natural loga­
rithmic transformation with an addition of one to these citation counts.

4.4. Heterogeneous analysis
4.4.1. Open access
In this section, we investigate whether OA introduces heterogeneity. As illustrated in Table 6, the coefficients of the independent 
variable in both models are significantly positive. However, the regression coefficient for the novelty of open access (OA) articles (β =
0.037; p < 0.01) is markedly higher than that for non-OA articles (β = 0.025; p < 0.01). The result indicates that, in OA articles, the 
impact of novelty on social attention is more pronounced. Our findings support previous research suggesting that in open access (OA) 
environments, the transparency and accessibility of research results are significantly enhanced. This environment provides favorable 
conditions for the dissemination of novel articles, enabling them to reach a broader audience and thus increase social attention. 
However, it is important to note that our study does not attempt to establish a direct causal relationship between novelty and social 
attention. Other factors may also contribute to higher social attention to articles.
4.4.2. Top journal
In this section, we examine whether papers published in top-tier journals achieve relative advantages. Drawing on previous 
research, we define the journals listed in FT50 and UT Dallas 24 as top-tier journals (Hou et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023). We use binary 
variables FT50 and UTD24 based on whether the papers are affiliated with the journals on the respective lists to reflect the effect of the 
top-tier journals. The results in Table 7 indicate that the advantages conferred by novelty are more likely to be recognized in papers 
from top-tier journals.
4.5. Additional analysis
In this section, we select specific metrics—Mendeley, Twitter, Blogs, News, Facebook, and Wikipedia—for further analysis of 
behavior in different contexts. Observations of the collected Altmetric data reveal varying coverage rates of sample mentions across 
different media sources. The data were grouped according to whether the papers were classified as novel, with specific coverage rates 
presented in Table 8. Both groups exhibit high mention rates on Mendeley and Twitter, consistent with previous statistical findings. 
Additionally, no significant distribution differences are observed between the two groups across all seven media sources, allowing for 
further analysis of the differential effects of novelty on social attention across various media platforms.
In accordance with the previously outlined methodology, we applied a plus-one natural logarithm transformation to the counts of 
various media sources. The results are presented in Table 9, indicating that all results are statistically significant in the positive di­
rection. The effect observed in Mendeley (β = 0.046; p = < 0.01) is particularly prominent, approximately 2.4 times that of Twitter and 
as much as ten more times greater than other media types. Mendeley is the most recently prominent data source, and its high coef­
ficient may be associated with the broadest reader coverage and its nature as an early indicator of academic impact (Zahedi et al., 
2017). The most extreme disparity is observed in Wikipedia (β = 0.002; p = < 0.01), which is believed to reflect the educational impact 
dimension of societal influence (Gonz´alez-Betancor & Dorta-Gonz´alez, 2023).

4. Discussion
The main objective of this study is to examine whether the novelty of a paper is associated with the social attention it receives on 
social media platforms. While many previous studies have explored the relationship between a paper’s novelty and its scientific 
impact, the social attention dimension remains underexplored, particularly in terms of empirical evidence for individual metrics. In 
this work, we focus on the impact of a paper’s novelty on the subsequent social attention it garners.
The findings of the current study indicate a significant positive relationship between the novelty of a paper and its social attention. 
Specifically, our results demonstrate that papers characterized by higher levels of novelty tend to receive greater social engagement on 
platforms. This aligns with the broader understanding that innovative research is more likely to capture public interest and attention. 
We conducted several robustness tests by replacing the variables and narrowing the sample scope; the results remained significant, 
even stronger in some cases. Heterogeneity tests further indicated that publications in top-tier journals and those with open access 
(OA) channels exhibited notable spillover effects. Finally, the source-specific analyses of Mendeley, Twitter, Blogs, News, Facebook, 
and Wikipedia showed that Mendeley and Twitter provided the strongest representations, which is consistent with the prior findings.
We propose several explanations for our findings. First, novel research often addresses underexplored issues that can stimulate 
curiosity within both academic and public audiences, thereby drawing greater attention. For instance, interdisciplinary studies may 
engage experts and audiences from multiple fields. Furthermore, users of social media platforms are inclined to share content that is 
unique and thought-provoking, and highly novel papers meet this criterion, thereby garnering greater attention on such platforms. 
Second, papers with high levels of novelty may be more frequently covered by the media or cited by science communicators, as 
journalists and communicators often prioritize research with groundbreaking findings for reporting. This external exposure further 
amplifies the influence of such papers on social media platforms. Additionally, public understanding of and interest in science is often 
shaped by media coverage; thus, highly novel papers, once disseminated through media channels, are more likely to spark widespread 
social discussion and engagement.
Also, this study acknowledges several limitations that warrant further investigation. First, our focus on economics and business 
papers raises the question of whether the findings would differ across other disciplines. Second, we analyze only journal articles; future 
studies could broaden this scope to include patents and project data, enhancing representativeness and comprehensiveness. Third, the 
article lacks more analysis using various alternative metrics to quantify the novelty of scientific papers. Thus, future research can 
explore a broader range of dynamics in this context.
5. Conclusion
In this study, we examined the relationship between the novelty of research papers and the level of social attention they garner. Our 
dataset includes over 310,000 journal articles in the economics and business field, published between 2012 and 2019. Of these, 
101,540 were categorized as novel and 214,764 as non-novel.
Our primary contribution lies in identifying the nuanced relationship between research novelty and its social attention, offering 
empirical evidence that underscores the unique role of truly novel work in driving both academic progress and societal interest. This 
paper offers insights into enhancing the public communication of scientific research and bridging the knowledge gap between science 
and society. Research characterized by higher novelty tends to receive greater social visibility and media attention, facilitating faster
